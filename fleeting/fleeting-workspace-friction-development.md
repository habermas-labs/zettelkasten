## The trigger

Kirsh (1995) frames workspace setup and continuous management as integral to thinking — spatial arrangements that simplify choice, perception, or internal computation. The framing opens a question Kirsh doesn't ask: what happens to workspace organization *across development*?

## The carpentry framing (Skooter's capture)

A beginner carpenter plausibly wants a minimal set of simple tools, easily within reach, so they don't have to spend thought on what they're doing. The master has specialized — a diverse toolset, and very possibly an idiosyncratic organization of the space governed not by external judgments of function or purpose but by their own workflow, the types of work they favor, and the methods they use to do it.

Both workspaces can be described as minimizing friction *for that specific user at that specific skill level*. But — the core claim — **for the novice to become a master, they must violate that norm. They have to cultivate productive friction to move from where they are to where they see themselves in the future.**

The sociocognitive question: how do we map the development from the novice's workspace to the master's workspace?

## Sharpening from Kirsh

Kirsh's "simplification" is always simplification of *cognition*, often purchased with additional physical action (the over-rotating Tetris player spends motoric effort to buy perceptual ease). So Kirsh was never describing friction-minimization in the workload-outsourcing sense — action is expended to serve internal work, not replace it. "Minimize friction" underdescribes even the fluent workspace; what gets minimized is a *specific kind* of friction. Which frictions to preserve is the developmental variable.

## Desirable difficulties

The established construct waiting for the carpentry framing: Bjork's desirable difficulties, and the Soderstrom & Bjork (2015) learning/performance dissociation (already Tier 1 in the acquisition cluster). A workspace tuned for maximal fluency at current skill optimizes *performance*; development requires arrangements that introduce difficulties desirable for growth. "Productive friction" (design language) and "desirable difficulties" (cognition language) are the same object viewed from two sides. This gives the norm-violation claim empirical grounding: it is the workspace-level instantiation of a documented learning principle.

## The apprenticeship move (the sociocultural prize)

Historically, the novice→master workspace transition did not happen by the novice redesigning their own space. It happened through apprenticeship: the novice worked in the *master's* workspace. Lave & Wenger's legitimate peripheral participation is exactly this — inhabiting an arrangement organized by someone else's expertise, where the friction experienced (tools not yet understood, organization serving a workflow not yet acquired) **is the curriculum**.

The master's idiosyncratic arrangement is sedimented cognitive history (Hutchins: the workspace as precomputed residue of practice). The apprentice develops by living inside that sediment until it becomes legible. The mapping from novice workspace to master workspace runs through **shared space**, not solo redesign.

## The AI-education implication

AI hands every learner a fully self-configured cognitive workspace from day one, with no master's shop to inhabit. The learner arranges everything for current-level comfort — precisely the arrangement that forecloses development. The friction curriculum isn't violated; **it never exists**.

CT read through this lens: an attempt to build a workspace where the friction is architectural rather than optional. Multi-model divergence, role rotation, the Conductor's deliberate pacing are spatial-dynamics-that-*complicate*-rather-than-simplify, chosen on purpose. CT as the master's shop the solo chat window removed — or more precisely, as a designed shared space whose arrangement encodes expertise the user hasn't yet acquired.

## Ethics of Becoming resonance

"Where they see themselves in the future": the developmental workspace is arranged not for who you are but for who you are becoming. Workspace design is never epistemically neutral — it encodes a commitment about trajectory.
