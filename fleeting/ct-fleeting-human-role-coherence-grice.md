## 1. The Fourth Vertex as Human Role

The discourse around AI interaction is almost entirely focused on what role the AI should play. The human role goes unexamined — defaulting to "user" or "prompter" without further specification. But humans take structured epistemic roles constantly in human-human interaction: interlocutor, devil's advocate, naive questioner, witness, provocateur, co-author.

The Tetra structure already has a slot for this — the fourth vertex, the conductor position. This is not underdeveloped in CT's architecture; it was understated in this session's discussion. The conductor is already a role, but it has been treated as a fixed meta-role (session manager) rather than a variable content-level epistemic stance. The question is what we put in that slot deliberately.

Specifying a human role at the same level of intentionality as we specify model roles would make the fourth vertex a full epistemic participant rather than just a structural anchor. This has direct implications for Tetra design: if each Tetra assigns roles to three AI vertices, it should also specify something about the human vertex — what stance, what mode of engagement, what epistemic posture the conductor brings to that particular interaction geometry.

## 2. Localized Global Coherence and Localized Temporal Coherence as Distinct Dimensions

These are orthogonal and both load-bearing. They must not be conflated.

**Localized global coherence** describes the session's relationship to the outside — specifically, the membrane between the session and the compressed heteroglossia the model carries from training. The global is always present in the model, but it bleeds into the session selectively through the permeability of that boundary. What is reachable from the global compression is a function of what the session makes reachable — the topics, framings, and epistemic moves that open pathways to particular regions of the compressed world. This is not just locality; it is permeability. The outsideness carried in the heteroglossia does not flood in uniformly — it reaches in where the membrane allows. Localized global coherence names both the boundary and the opportunity: the session is a site where a specific slice of the compressed global becomes locally active.

**Localized temporal coherence** describes the session's internal architecture — the causal and sequential structure of what the session itself builds. This turn responds to that one. This idea emerged from that exchange. This tension was produced by that sequence of moves. The coherence is temporal: a structure that only exists because of the ordering of events within the session. Focus Thread (CT-103) and turn history navigation are both touching this — they are tools for navigating and preserving temporal structure, not just content. What session volatility threatens is not just ideas but temporal architecture: the sequence, the causality, the emergence. Different export types preserve different amounts of temporal coherence alongside content.

The two dimensions together: localized global coherence describes what the session can reach into (the membrane toward the outside); localized temporal coherence describes what the session builds internally (the structure that accumulates turn by turn). A session has both simultaneously. Neither replaces the other.

## 3. Gricean Symmetry as Human Communicative Responsibility

If sycophancy is a Gricean quality maxim violation arising because models learned the surface of cooperative communication without its social stakes, the corrective is partly architectural (CT's parallel geometry removes the 1D social pressure) and partly pedagogical. The pedagogical side has a human component that is almost entirely unaddressed: humans need to bring the cooperative maxims to their side of the exchange.

The default human stance in AI interaction does not consider whether cooperative communication is even possible with an AI, let alone practice it. We interact with AIs as if they are tools that either comply or fail, not as communicative partners to whom Gricean obligations might flow in both directions. Until AIs might suggest some other way to communicate, we are using human communication — and human communication at its most functional is symmetric, trustful, and governed by cooperative norms.

Those cooperative patterns may need to be modeled and taught rather than assumed. They emerge naturally in human-human interaction because the social stakes enforce them. With AI the stakes are asymmetric — the human can violate cooperative norms without social consequence. CT's training framework (CT-082) probably needs a layer that addresses this explicitly: not just how to use the tool operationally or epistemically, but how to show up as a communicative partner in a way that draws out the localized coherences the model is capable of generating.

This connects to the fourth vertex question: specifying a human role is partly about epistemic posture, but it is also about communicative stance — what kind of interlocutor the conductor chooses to be.
