## The core claim

The mode of AI use perceived to produce an unearned, maximally successful outcome is very likely the worst case in truth. The participation choice that *looks* like maximally successful AI use is actually the deepest surrender of agency, with real learning and ethical consequences.

## The two-pole scenario

Current educational context:

- Student: "AI, write my paper for me"
- Teacher: "We have to stop students from cheating with AI"

Both poles tap the omniscient-epistemic-authority perception (Lindebaum). There is a shared belief that the student can "do" stellar work this way — from both the student's and the teacher's view. This is the minimal-friction case: the student surrenders agency (workload outsourcing). In truth, output quality is a crapshoot ("slop" risk) and no learning occurs.

Where we want to be:

- Student: "AI, help me write a good paper"
- Teacher: (even considering this framing would be aspirational for most)

This is the increasing-friction case — student agency exerted. Quality of work *and* of learning is a function in part of the agency the student exerts. Open question: without the required distributed metacognition, is this possibility even perceived? And if perceived, is it dismissed as too hard, past the point of diminishing returns?

## The architecture of the irony

The irony is a **perception-reality inversion at both the student and teacher pole simultaneously**.

The low-friction case is perceived as an asymptote: maximally successful output, minimally costly. From outside (teacher) and inside (student), it reads as the ideal game move. What actually happens at each layer:

- Output quality is a crapshoot (slop risk)
- Learning is zero — not merely suppressed but never initiated; the metacognitive trigger never fires (no disfluency → no metacognitive loop; Alter et al. / distributed-metacognition material)
- The work is not the student's in any meaningfully defensible sense

The perceived best case is empirically the likely worst case on every dimension except one narrow metric: time cost to submission. The irony: it is only optimal on the dimension everyone implicitly assumes is a proxy for all the others — and it isn't.

The high-friction case is perceived as harder (student: more effort), less reliable (teacher: still suspects AI involvement), and often not conceived as a distinct option at all, because most educators are running a binary — AI-cheating vs. no-AI. That binary is itself a finite-game frame applied to something that can only pay out in infinite-game terms (Carse). Treating AI as a new productivity technology remains a low-friction perspective focused on the finite game of "complete your assignment" instead of the infinite game of "sit with that thought and work with it."

## Theoretical connections

**Dewey flip.** Dewey inverted the relationship between experience and education — education isn't preparation for experience; experience is the site of education. The parallel flip here: output isn't the proxy for learning; output produced *through* the learning process is the **residue** of learning, not its evidence. The AI-writes version produces output without the residue-generating process.

**Lindebaum / omniscient epistemic authority.** Both poles credit AI with epistemic power it structurally lacks. The student trusts AI to produce authority-passing work; the teacher fears AI as capable of producing undetectable authority-passing work. Both are attributing the situated, localized judgment that makes work *good* rather than merely *plausible*. Connects to the heteroglossia-compression argument: what the AI generates is probabilistically plausible, not situated-specifically-true.

**Workload outsourcing vs. cognitive offloading.** The low-friction case is workload outsourcing in its purest form — not cognitive offloading, because no cognition is being assisted; none is occurring. Cognitive offloading is a question of degree; workload outsourcing is a categorical bypass of the learning process. (Distinction planted in LinkedIn Article 1 as a single sentence; needs proper development somewhere in the vault — possibly in workload-outsourcing.md itself.)

**Distributed metacognition.** The perception question is load-bearing: perceiving the high-friction option as an option at all may require the distributed metacognitive capacity that low-friction use forecloses. Potential vicious circle worth developing.

## Naming

"Irony of Agency" retained, with a noted ambiguity: it can read as irony *about* agency (topic) or irony *produced by* agency choices (mechanism). The mechanism reading is the intended one. Alternatives considered: "Agency Inversion," "The Optimality Illusion." Keeping "irony" because the perception-reality gap is dramatic enough to deserve the word.
