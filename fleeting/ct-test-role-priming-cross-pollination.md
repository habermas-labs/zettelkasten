Core question: Does prior role experience improve subsequent role performance in AI models? Does inhabiting the Skeptic role make a model a better Case-maker, as Gemini's cross-pollination argument suggests?

Origin: Gemini's observation in the Fundraiser Tetra triangulation session (2026-03-13) that role rotation may foster "cross-pollination and empathy" — a former Skeptic may make a more robust Case-maker because they have internalized the objection structure. This is empirically testable.

Gaming analogy (holy trinity — tank/healer/DPS): knowing how the other roles function within a game improves your own performance. A healer who has tanked knows exactly what the tank needs at the pull and stops waiting to be told. The knowledge transfers as anticipatory modeling, not just information. This is an empirical finding about role interdependence in complex collaborative systems.

Experimental design:
- Run a model through the Skeptic role for a few turns on a throwaway topic
- Switch it to Case-maker on the real topic
- Compare output to a cold Case-maker on the same topic
- If primed version produces more objection-anticipating case, cross-pollination effect is real and operable

Warm-up hypothesis: prior role experience may function as a warm-up exercise — not memory in any persistent sense, but priming. The role prompt activates associated patterns — objection structures, vulnerability awareness, the shape of what needs defending. Testable mechanism: priming activates associated discourse patterns from training, not session memory.

Implication for Tetra design: if cross-pollination is real, deliberate role sequencing becomes a design variable. Which role should precede which for optimal output? Does the sequence matter or only the exposure?

Implication for training: if cross-pollination improves AI role performance, it may also improve human role performance in CT-mediated sessions — a conductor who has inhabited each role understands its constraints and failure modes in a way that purely observational experience doesn't provide. Connects to Palincsar's Reciprocal Teaching design principle.
