Prompted by a radio segment on "friction maxing" as a cultural trend — intentional inconvenience as a response to tech-driven friction removal.

Three threads worth developing:

**Frugality as phenomenological discipline.** Most people who practice frugality describe it in instrumental terms: saving money, simplicity, not needing much. The phenomenological account — that frugality restores the weight of decisions, reintroduces the In-Between into economic life, requires dwelling in the question of whether something is worth it — is not how practitioners typically narrate it. The practice may be doing genuine epistemic work that the practitioner is not consciously aware of. This is the gap between folk wisdom and philosophical account. The question of whether naming that account changes the practice (for better or worse) is open.

**Businesses and friction removal.** The dominant design logic of consumer technology is friction minimization: one-click purchase, instant streaming, frictionless checkout. This is optimization for Layer 1 (operational) at the expense of the conditions that make Layer 2 and Layer 3 possible. The temporal asynchrony of AI — instantaneous availability — is the extreme case. When friction is the site where intention lives, removing it may hollow out the meaning of the transaction even while improving its efficiency. The movie mattered more when you had to go get it.

**Friction maxing as domesticated resistance.** The gut-level instinct toward productive friction is real. Cultural trends like friction maxing, mindfulness, slow living, analog intentionality — these exist because something genuine is being sensed. But the instinct alone gets you only as far as the culturally available version, which is typically already domesticated: the friction-generating practice delivered without its genuine restructuring requirement. Giving up something you can easily do without is not maxing anything. Cold plunges and dopamine fasts are real practices but they tend to become the point of themselves — performative action rather than a path toward somewhere. The action becomes the identity marker. The original signal (friction can be productive) gets packaged and sold back as lifestyle optimization.

The deeper problem: naming a practice accelerates its domestication. Once it has a name, it can be marketed, and marketing removes the friction. "Friction maxing" as a named trend is already in the process of becoming its own domesticated form.

**Open question:** Is there a version of this that doesn't domesticate? What would it look like to spread the genuine practice rather than the packaged version? This connects to the CT problem: how do you make something accessible enough to reach people without stripping out the part that actually does the work?

**Csikszentmihalyi connection (personal):** Heard him speak at AERA, early 1990s. He opened by addressing the pronunciation of his name — "just call me Mike" — which is itself a small demonstration of wu wei: no forced performance, effortless resolution of a friction point that doesn't matter. Flow as the phenomenological account of Piaget's optimal equilibration. Connected at the time to Heidegger's ready-to-hand as complementary accounts of the same underlying state approached from phenomenological vs. ontological vantage points.
