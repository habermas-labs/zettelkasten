## Core Concepts

### Lived Hermeneutics (Packer)

Martin Packer's account of hermeneutics, drawing on Heidegger, situates interpretation not as a cognitive act performed on a text but as a condition of being-in-the-world. We do not first exist and then interpret — we exist *as* interpreters. Understanding is not something we do; it is something we are always already doing by virtue of being situated creatures.

This is hermeneutics as lived practice rather than method.

### The Horizon

Heidegger's concept of the horizon designates the boundary of what is visible from a given situated position. The horizon is not a limitation to be overcome — it is the condition of any understanding at all. To be somewhere is to have a horizon. To have a horizon is to see some things clearly, others obliquely, and some not at all.

Critically: the horizon moves with the observer. Change your location and the projection of every object in the world changes with it. The object has not changed. The relationship between observer and object has — and that relationship is constitutive of what appears.

### Projection into the World

An object's meaning is not contained in the object itself. It is the object's *projection* into the world from a particular situated vantage point. Different vantage points generate different projections — not different opinions about a fixed meaning, but genuinely different appearances of the same thing. The projections are all real. None is complete.

This is the structural reason why a single perspective cannot exhaust the meaning of anything. The object always exceeds what any one horizon can disclose.

## Connection to Outsideness (vnenakhodimost')

Bakhtin's outsideness (*vnye-nah-KHOH-dee-most'*) and Packer/Heidegger's horizon are complementary accounts of the same underlying structure, approached from different traditions.

Bakhtin emphasizes the *productive value* of the external position — what the outside observer gains that the inside participant cannot have. Packer/Heidegger emphasize the *mechanism* — why position change produces genuinely different understanding rather than merely different opinion.

Together they form a complete argument:
- The horizon explains *why* different positions disclose different things (mechanism)
- Outsideness explains *what* the external position specifically gains (value)

Neither alone is sufficient. The horizon without outsideness gives you relativism — all positions equally partial, no privileged value to any. Outsideness without the horizon gives you a strong claim about external value without a structural account of why it holds. Together they justify the design of CT.

## Application to Multi-Model Triangulation

CT is a practical instantiation of this theoretical structure. Each model occupies a different position in the space of trained interpretive dispositions — a different situated horizon. Their responses to the same prompt are not different opinions; they are different projections of the prompt's meaning into the world from different vantage points.

The implication is significant: the divergences between model responses are not noise to be averaged out. They are the signal. Each divergence marks a place where the object's projection differs across horizons — which is precisely where the object's complexity lives.

Triangulation in CT is the practice of holding multiple projections simultaneously in order to see the object more fully than any single horizon permits. This is not synthesis in the sense of finding a single correct view. It is the maintenance of productive multiplicity — using the differences to map the shape of the thing being examined.

## The Tetrahedron as Diagram

The CT logo — a regular tetrahedron with one vertex per model and one for the user — is a geometric expression of this hermeneutic argument. Each vertex is an equivalent situated position. No vertex is privileged by the geometry. The object under discussion (whatever the session prompt addresses) is the implicit center — visible differently from each vertex, never fully from any one.

The choice of a *regular* tetrahedron, with all edges equal and all faces equilateral triangles, is a design decision with philosophical content: geometric equivalence enforces the claim that no position is structurally superior. Any apparent hierarchy (the "top" vertex in a fixed 2D view) is an artifact of the viewing angle, not a property of the shape. Rotating the tetrahedron dissolves the illusion.
