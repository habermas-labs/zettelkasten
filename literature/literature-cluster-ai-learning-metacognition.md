## Tier 1 — Highest Priority

**Risko, E. F., & Gilbert, S. J. (2016).** Cognitive offloading. *Trends in Cognitive Sciences, 20*(9), 676–688.
Status: `to-acquire`
Fan et al. cite this as the mechanism underlying metacognitive laziness — learners delegating cognitive tasks to external tools, leading to decreased internal engagement over time. Needs direct inspection: their use implicitly treats offloading as harmful, which sits in tension with Clark & Chalmers' Extended Mind Thesis, from which the cognitive offloading literature derives. EMT treats tool-extended cognition as a genuine expansion of the cognitive system, not a degradation of it. The question is whether Risko & Gilbert themselves make this slide or whether Fan et al. introduce it. Central to the workload-outsourcing critique and the distributed metacognition argument.

**Soderstrom, N. C., & Bjork, R. A. (2015).** Learning versus performance: An integrative review. *Perspectives on Psychological Science, 10*(2), 176–199.
Status: `to-acquire`
Theoretical foundation for Fan et al.'s key dissociation finding: ChatGPT improves essay performance but not knowledge gain or transfer. The learning/performance distinction is load-bearing — it is what makes the finding interesting rather than just "AI helps students." Also directly relevant to CT's argument that optimizing AI deployment for task output is the wrong target; learning in the Soderstrom & Bjork sense is what CT is designed to protect and cultivate.

**Alter, A. L., Oppenheimer, D. M., Epley, N., & Eyre, R. N. (2007).** Overcoming intuition: Metacognitive difficulty activates analytic reasoning. *Journal of Experimental Psychology: General, 136*(4), 569–576.
Status: `to-acquire`
The mechanistic paper Fan et al. actually rely on most heavily, though Risko & Gilbert gets more explicit billing. The argument: metacognitive disfluency — the experience of cognitive difficulty — triggers System 2 / analytical processing. Remove the difficulty (as generative AI does by producing satisfying outputs) and the trigger doesn't fire. This is the foundation of the trigger non-activation critique: Fan et al.'s own cited mechanism defeats their "laziness" construct. Laziness requires the trigger to fire and the response to be withheld; trigger non-activation means the loop never opens.

**Järvelä, S., Nguyen, A., & Hadwin, A. (2023).** Human and artificial intelligence collaboration for socially shared regulation in learning. *British Journal of Educational Technology, 54*(5), 1057–1076.
Status: `to-acquire`
Most directly CT-adjacent citation in the Fan et al. reference list. Socially shared regulation in learning (SSRL) extended to human-AI collaboration — the zone CT operates in theoretically. Järvelä is a major figure in SRL research; Hadwin has done foundational work on co-regulation and SSRL. This is the state of the art in the field CT is entering and needs to be read carefully for both convergence and differentiation.

## Tier 2 — High Priority

**Molenaar, I. (2022a).** The concept of hybrid human-AI regulation: Exemplifying how to support young learners' self-regulated learning. *Computers and Education: Artificial Intelligence, 3*, 100070.
Status: `to-acquire`
One of two Molenaar papers Fan et al. draw on heavily for the hybrid intelligence framing. Molenaar is constructing the theoretical framework Fan et al. operationalize. "Hybrid human-AI regulation" is the closest existing construct to what CT theorizes; need to understand precisely where her framework ends and where distributed metacognition begins. Note: *Computers and Education: AI* is open access — may be freely available.

**Molenaar, I. (2022b).** Towards hybrid human-AI learning technologies. *European Journal of Education, 57*(4), 632–645.
Status: `to-acquire`
Companion paper. Together with 2022a, defines the theoretical space of hybrid human-AI learning that CT is entering. The two Molenaar papers and the Järvelä et al. paper above are the three sources most likely to define CT's theoretical neighbors and differentiation points.

**de Bruin, A. B., Biwer, F., Hui, L., Onan, E., David, L., & Wiradhany, W. (2023).** Worth the effort: The start and stick to desirable difficulties (s2d2) framework. *Educational Psychology Review, 35*(2), 41.
Status: `to-acquire`
Formalizes desirable difficulties as an explicit pedagogical framework — the theoretical legitimation for CT's friction design choices. The argument that difficulty and resistance are features of effective learning environments, not bugs to be engineered away, is exactly the counterargument to uncritical AI-smoothing of learning experience. "Start and stick" framing is also relevant to the question of how distributed metacognition as a skill gets built and maintained.

**Holstein, K., Aleven, V., & Rummel, N. (2020).** A conceptual framework for human-AI hybrid adaptivity in education. In *Artificial Intelligence in Education: AIED 2020* (pp. 240–254). Springer.
Status: `to-acquire`
Framework paper for hybrid adaptivity in educational AI specifically. Complements Molenaar and Järvelä; fills out the AIED research context CT is entering. Aleven is a major figure in intelligent tutoring systems; this paper's position in the hybrid intelligence trajectory is worth understanding.

## Tier 3 — Worth Acquiring When Possible

**Paoli, S. D. (2024).** Performing an inductive thematic analysis of semi-structured interviews with a large language model: An exploration and provocation on the limits of the approach. *Social Science Computer Review, 42*(4), 997–1019.
Status: `to-acquire`
Methodological bridge between qualitative research and LLM assistance. Relevant at two levels: substantively, because studying distributed metacognition will require qualitative methods that behavioral trace data cannot reach; meta-methodologically, because it critically examines what AI can and cannot do in the interpretive layer of qualitative analysis. The "exploration and provocation on the limits" framing suggests genuine critical engagement rather than promotion.

**Noy, S., & Zhang, W. (2023).** Experimental evidence on the productivity effects of generative artificial intelligence. *Science, 381*(6654), 187–192.
Status: `to-acquire`
Flagship empirical paper on ChatGPT productivity effects in professional contexts. Widely cited; useful background for the asymmetry flattening / AI-as-bad-human argument and the sophisticated AI slop prediction. May be open access — check before acquiring.

**Steiss, J., et al. (2024).** Comparing the quality of human and ChatGPT feedback on students' writing. *Learning and Instruction, 91*, 101894.
Status: `to-acquire`
Direct empirical comparison of human vs. AI feedback quality on student writing. Relevant to CT's claim that multi-model triangulation produces something qualitatively different from single-model interaction, and to the AI-as-bad-human argument about tasks that appear AI-tractable but require human judgment for their substance.

## Freely Available — No Acquisition Needed

**Akata, Z., et al. (2020).** A research agenda for hybrid intelligence. *Computer, 53*(8), 18–28. https://doi.org/10.1109/MC.2020.2996587
Status: `to-acquire` — IEEE open access; download directly.
Founding document of hybrid intelligence as a named research field. 27-author research agenda from Dutch universities (Amsterdam, Delft, Twente); published 2020. Positions HI explicitly as alternative to AGI pursuit. Definition — "augmenting human intellect and capabilities instead of replacing them" — is structurally human-centric in ways the integrated intelligence framing challenges. Essential for understanding what field CT is entering and differentiating from.

**Steinert, S., Avila, K. E., Ruzika, S., Kuhn, J., & Küchemann, S. (2023).** Harnessing large language models to enhance self-regulated learning via formative feedback. arXiv:2311.1398
Status: `to-acquire` — freely available at arxiv.org
LLMs applied specifically to SRL support via formative feedback. Relevant as an empirical instance of the design approach CT theorizes against — AI providing feedback that forecloses rather than opens metacognitive engagement.
