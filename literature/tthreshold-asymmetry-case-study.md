## Sources

---

### Rogers, Carl R. — *On Becoming a Person* (1961)

**Role in case study:** Grounds the Witness role and the limit case argument simultaneously.

Rogers' three core conditions for therapeutic change — unconditional positive regard, empathy, and congruence — provide the theoretical frame for why the AI Witness can instantiate one condition structurally (unconditional positive regard: no ego-protective machinery, no face-saving, no status monitoring) while being structurally unable to provide another (congruence: genuine presence as a self in contact with the patient's self). This is not a criticism of AI — it is a precise mapping of the asymmetry. The Witness role is Rogerian in its receiving function; the therapeutic relationship requires Rogers' full triad and cannot be reduced to one condition.

The ego asymmetry is the mechanism: the AI's lack of ego-protective machinery is what makes unconditional positive regard structurally available rather than effortfully achieved. A human therapist must work to bracket their own reactions; the AI does not have the reactions to bracket.

**Key texts:** *On Becoming a Person* (1961); *Client-Centered Therapy* (1951); *A Way of Being* (1980)

**Connection to other sources:** Rogers' congruence condition is the complement to van der Kolk's social surveillance argument — both point to what is irreplaceable about embodied human presence in clinical relationship.

---

### van der Kolk, Bessel — *The Body Keeps the Score* (2014)

**Role in case study:** Grounds the Witness role's specific utility for patients with trauma histories; extends the ego asymmetry argument into the clinical psychology of narration.

Van der Kolk's research on trauma and narrative capacity is directly relevant to the accommodation-seeking population. Trauma disrupts the capacity to narrate — specifically, it disrupts narration under conditions of social surveillance, where the narrator is monitoring the listener's reaction while speaking. Shame, dismissal, and re-traumatization in prior clinical encounters are common in this population. The AI Witness removes the social surveillance condition structurally: the patient is not watched while they speak. This may be the condition that makes narration possible for patients who have been unable to narrate their experience in prior encounters.

The privacy architecture of CT compounds this: no sign-in, no data persistence, session disappears on close. The privacy contract is not just a practical feature — for patients with trauma histories, it may be the safety condition that enables the work.

**Key texts:** *The Body Keeps the Score* (2014)

**Note:** Van der Kolk's work has been the subject of methodological debate in trauma research; cite for the clinical observation about narration under surveillance rather than for specific neurobiological claims.

### Herman, Judith — *Trauma and Recovery* (1992)

**Role in case study:** Provides the three-stage recovery framework (safety, remembrance and mourning, reconnection) as a map for where The Threshold session operates; introduces *testimony* as the concept for the talking points document.

Herman's three stages — establishing safety, remembrance and mourning, reconnection with ordinary life and community — give The Threshold a location in the recovery arc. The session operates at stages one and three: creating enough safety for narration (the Witness condition), and reconnecting the narration to the social world through self-advocacy (the Scribe condition). Stage two (remembrance and mourning) is not the session's work — and the design should be careful not to pull toward it.

Herman's concept of *testimony* — the survivor's account as simultaneously therapeutic act and social document — is the deepest framing for what the talking points document is. It is not just a preparation tool; it is an act of making the experience legible and recognized in the social world. This gives the document moral weight beyond its instrumental function.

**Key texts:** *Trauma and Recovery* (1992)

### Bruner, Jerome — *Actual Minds, Possible Worlds* (1986); *Making Stories* (2002)

**Role in case study:** Names the translation the Scribe performs as a translation between narrative modes — from story (the patient's account) to case (the institutional form).

Bruner's distinction between narrative and paradigmatic (logico-scientific) modes of thought is directly applicable. The patient arrives with a story: a lived, temporally structured, personally meaningful account of their experience. The institution requires a case: categorized, evidence-supported, frameable within established procedures. These are not just different registers — they are different cognitive modes with different logics of proof and different criteria for truth.

The Scribe manages the translation between modes without collapsing the story into the case. The Witness's prior reception — receiving the story on its own terms before translation begins — is what preserves the story's fidelity through translation. Bruner also gives language for what can go wrong when the translation happens too quickly: the story is lost, and what remains is a generic case that no longer matches the patient's actual experience.

Bruner bridges the educational and clinical traditions, making his work unusually legible across both audiences.

**Key texts:** *Actual Minds, Possible Worlds* (1986); *Making Stories* (2002); *Acts of Meaning* (1990)

### Bourdieu, Pierre — *The Logic of Practice* (1990); *Language and Symbolic Power* (1991)

**Role in case study:** Names what the Navigator is doing as capital transfer; explains why patients without institutional language are not less valid but less legible.

Bourdieu's field theory provides the sociological vocabulary for the power differential the patient is navigating. Every institutional field (medicine, HR, legal, insurance) has its own form of cultural capital — the right language, the right framing, the right documentation — that determines whether a claim is legible and taken seriously. Patients who arrive at accommodation encounters without that capital are not dismissed because their experience is less real; they are dismissed because they cannot present it in a form the field recognizes.

The Navigator is a temporary capital transfer: it gives the patient enough field-specific capital to be heard in this encounter, without pretending to give them permanent mastery of the field or eliminating the structural inequality. This is an honest framing of what the session can and cannot do.

Bourdieu's concept of *habitus* — the embodied dispositions that make field navigation feel natural to those who have been socialized into it — explains why the capital transfer is not just a matter of giving the patient the right words. The practitioner or HR professional navigates their field through habitus; the patient is navigating it through explicitly acquired knowledge. The gap remains. The Navigator reduces it enough for this encounter.

**Key texts:** *The Logic of Practice* (1990); *Language and Symbolic Power* (1991); *Distinction* (1984) for field and capital concepts

### Freire, Paulo — *Pedagogy of the Oppressed* (1970)

**Role in case study:** Provides the ethical frame for the patient's authority over their own experience; names what the session is doing as conscientization — not giving the patient knowledge they lack, but giving them tools to name what they already know.

Freire's conscientization (*conscientização*) is the process of developing critical consciousness — learning to perceive one's situation not as fixed reality but as a situation that can be named, analyzed, and acted upon. The Threshold session is conscientization in this sense: the patient already knows their experience; the session gives them the tools to name it in a language the world will recognize. The AI is not the authority. The patient is the epistemic authority on their own situation; the AI is the scale and the translator.

This also grounds the ethical structure of the workload outsourcing argument: the labor moves (naming, translating, navigating the institutional terrain), but the judgment stays (whether the translation is true, whether the document represents the patient's actual experience). Freire would recognize this as the difference between banking education (depositing knowledge into a passive recipient) and genuine conscientization (enabling the person's own naming practice).

**Key texts:** *Pedagogy of the Oppressed* (1970); *Education for Critical Consciousness* (1974)

### Goffman, Erving — *The Presentation of Self in Everyday Life* (1959); *Interaction Ritual* (1967)

**Role in case study:** Grounds the ego asymmetry argument in social psychology; names face-work as the mechanism the AI Witness does not perform.

Goffman's dramaturgical framework — the management of self-presentation in social encounters — names precisely what the AI Witness is not doing. Face-work is the ongoing management of how one appears to others, protecting one's own face and attending to the face needs of the other. In a human intake encounter, both parties are performing face-work simultaneously. The patient is managing how they appear (not too demanding, not too fragile, credible, sympathetic); the clinician or HR professional is managing their own face as a competent, fair, responsive professional. This mutual face-work shapes what is said and what is withheld.

The AI Witness has no face to protect and makes no face demands on the patient. This is not just an absence of pressure — it is the structural condition for honest narration. The patient does not need to perform for the AI. They can describe their experience without managing how it lands.

Goffman's *stigma* concept (from *Stigma: Notes on the Management of Spoiled Identity*, 1963) is also relevant for the accommodation-seeking population: many patients with disabilities, mental health conditions, or chronic illness have extensive experience managing stigmatized identity in clinical and workplace settings. The AI encounter removes the stigma management requirement.

**Key texts:** *The Presentation of Self in Everyday Life* (1959); *Interaction Ritual* (1967); *Stigma* (1963)

### Antonovsky, Aaron — *Health, Stress, and Coping* (1979); *Unraveling the Mystery of Health* (1987)

**Role in case study:** Provides the salutogenic outcome framework; his sense of coherence construct maps onto what a successful Threshold session produces.

Antonovsky's salutogenic orientation asks not what causes illness but what enables people to maintain health and navigate stress. His key construct, *sense of coherence* (SOC), has three components: comprehensibility (the situation is understandable and predictable), manageability (I have the resources to cope), and meaningfulness (engagement with the challenge is worthwhile). These are resistance resources that enable navigation of stressors rather than avoidance.

A successful Threshold session should increase all three: comprehensibility (the patient understands the institutional terrain and what is required), manageability (they have a document that represents their experience effectively), and meaningfulness (the advocacy work is worth doing). The SOC framework suggests a potential outcome measure for evaluating whether Threshold sessions work as designed — not just whether a document was produced, but whether the patient's sense of coherence in relation to the upcoming encounter increased.

**Key texts:** *Health, Stress, and Coping* (1979); *Unraveling the Mystery of Health* (1987)

**Note:** Antonovsky is less widely known than the other sources in this note; introduce with brief context when citing for general audiences.

### Vygotsky, L.S. — *Mind in Society* (1978)

**Role in case study:** Grounds the scaffolding argument; ZPD as the theoretical frame for what the AI enables the patient to do that they cannot do alone.

The Zone of Proximal Development is the space between what a learner can do independently and what they can do with scaffolded support. The Threshold session is a ZPD instantiation: the patient can produce the practitioner document *with* AI scaffolding that they could not produce alone — not because they lack knowledge of their own experience, but because they lack the institutional capital (Bourdieu) and the translation tools (Bruner) to render it legible. The scaffold enables the crossing; the goal is that the patient arrives at the practitioner encounter more capable than they arrived at the session.

Vygotsky is already in CT's theoretical lineage (Reciprocal Teaching Tetra, sociocultural hermeneutics). His presence here is consistent and earns its place specifically through the ZPD application to self-advocacy.

The membrane concept — between local and global coherence — is also deeply Vygotskian: his entire project was about the interface between the individual's internal development and the cultural-historical tools available in the social world. The Threshold session is where those two coherences are forced into productive contact.

**Key texts:** *Mind in Society* (1978); *Thought and Language* (1934/1986)

---

### Bakhtin, M.M. — *The Dialogic Imagination* (1981); *Problems of Dostoevsky's Poetics* (1984)

**Role in case study:** Grounds the membrane as heteroglossia; names the centripetal forces of institutional language that the Witness must resist.

The patient's voice and the institutional voice are not merely different registers — they are what Bakhtin would call different *social languages*, each carrying its own world of values, assumptions, and power relations. The institutional language exerts centripetal force: it tends to absorb and normalize utterances that enter its field, translating them into its own terms. The Witness role is specifically anti-centripetal: it receives the patient's utterance on the patient's terms *before* the centripetal force of institutional language begins to reshape it.

The Scribe's translation is the moment of dialogic contact between the two social languages. The goal is not to eliminate the patient's voice but to find the utterance that can exist in both — true to the patient's experience and legible to the institution. This is the dialogic imagination applied to self-advocacy.

**Key texts:** *The Dialogic Imagination* (1981); *Problems of Dostoevsky's Poetics* (1984); *Speech Genres and Other Late Essays* (1986)

## Notes on Use

These sources span clinical psychology, educational theory, sociology, and philosophy. When citing for general audiences, the most immediately resonant names are Rogers, van der Kolk, and Freire. For academic or research audiences, Bourdieu, Bruner, and Antonovsky add theoretical precision. Bakhtin and Vygotsky are already established in CT's theoretical lineage and will be most legible to readers familiar with CT's broader framework.

Herman and van der Kolk should be introduced with care in contexts where the patient audience may have trauma histories — the goal is to explain the design rationale, not to center trauma in every description of the session.
