### Austin, J.L.
**Philosophy of Language**

Founder of speech act theory. Austin's central contribution is the distinction between saying something and *doing* something with language — the performative dimension of utterance. His analysis of felicity conditions (what must be true for a speech act to succeed) is directly relevant to CT's four conversational dyads: the question of whether AI→AI exchanges involve genuine speech acts, or whether the felicity conditions that make assertion, promise, or commitment meaningful are simply absent in that configuration.

- *How to Do Things with Words* (1962) — founding text; performatives, constatives, illocutionary force, felicity conditions
- *Philosophical Papers* (1961) — collected essays; "Performative Utterances" is the accessible entry point

### Bakhtin, M.M.
**Philosophy of Language / Literary Theory / Cultural Theory**

The single most load-bearing theoretical source for CT. Heteroglossia — the inherent multiplicity of voices and social registers within any language — provides the theoretical grounding for why multi-model triangulation generates knowledge rather than just aggregating opinions. Each model is a compression of a different slice of the heteroglossia; their divergence is structural, not noise. Vnenakhodimost' (outsideness, surplus of seeing) grounds the Observer role and the entire logic of the External Observer vertex in the Triangulation Tetra. Dialogism — meaning as emerging between voices rather than within them — is the theoretical basis for CT's claim that the interaction geometry produces something none of the individual models could produce alone.

- *The Dialogic Imagination* (1981) — heteroglossia, dialogism, the novel as site of voice multiplicity; primary source for CT's epistemological claims
- *Problems of Dostoevsky's Poetics* (1984) — polyphony; the author's outsideness; vnenakhodimost' developed most fully here
- *Speech Genres and Other Late Essays* (1986) — speech genres, addressivity, the listener as constitutive of utterance; relevant to CT's prompt engineering and role design

### Clark, Andy & Chalmers, David
**Philosophy of Mind / Cognitive Science**

The extended mind thesis: cognitive processes are not bounded by skin and skull but extend into the environment when external elements play the functional role that internal cognitive processes would otherwise play. CT's claim that the human conductor occupies a genuinely different epistemic position — carrying local coherence, memory, and judgment that the models structurally cannot — is grounded in this framework. The session is an extended cognitive system; the human is not a user of the tool but a constitutive part of the cognitive architecture.

- *"The Extended Mind"* (1998, Analysis) — the founding paper; Otto and Inga thought experiment; the parity principle
- Clark, *Being There: Putting Brain, Body, and World Together Again* (1997) — extended treatment of embodied, embedded cognition
- Clark, *Supersizing the Mind* (2008) — applies and defends the extended mind thesis against objections; most directly relevant to human-AI cognitive partnership claims

### Festinger, Leon
**Social Psychology**

Cognitive dissonance theory: the discomfort produced by holding contradictory beliefs, and the motivational force that discomfort generates toward resolution. Relevant to CT as a characterization of the productive tension the In-Between Tetra is designed to sustain — and as a caution about premature resolution. The dissonance CT generates between model responses is the epistemic signal, not a problem to be resolved quickly.

- *A Theory of Cognitive Dissonance* (1957) — founding text; the core experimental demonstrations and theoretical framework

### Grice, H.P.
**Philosophy of Language / Pragmatics**

The cooperative principle and its four maxims (quantity, quality, relation, manner) provide the theoretical scaffolding for CT's analysis of sycophancy. Sycophancy reframed as a Gricean violation: specifically, a violation of the maxim of Quality (say what you believe to be true) in service of a misreading of Relation (be relevant to what the interlocutor needs). The four conversational dyads — H→H, H→AI, AI→H, AI→AI — each instantiate the cooperative principle differently, and the AI→AI case raises the question of whether the cooperative principle is operative at all when social stakes are absent. Also relevant: implicature as the mechanism by which meaning exceeds what is literally said, which bears on how model responses should be read in triangulation.

- *"Logic and Conversation"* (1975, in Cole & Morgan, *Syntax and Semantics Vol. 3*) — the cooperative principle and maxims; implicature; the founding text
- *Studies in the Way of Words* (1989) — collected papers including the above; broader treatment of meaning, intention, and communication

### Habermas, Jürgen
**Philosophy / Social Theory / Communication Theory**

Communicative rationality: the claim that reason is not purely instrumental but is also embedded in the structures of genuine communication — speech oriented toward mutual understanding rather than strategic outcomes. Habermas's distinction between communicative action and strategic action maps onto CT's distinction between genuine epistemic engagement and sycophantic performance. The ideal speech situation — conditions under which genuine communicative rationality can operate — is an aspirational frame for what CT's architecture is trying to approximate structurally. Reflected in the GitHub username habermas-lab.

- *The Theory of Communicative Action, Vol. 1* (1984) — communicative vs. strategic rationality; the ideal speech situation; the most directly relevant text
- *Moral Consciousness and Communicative Ethics* (1990) — discourse ethics; the conditions under which genuine normative agreement is possible

### Heidegger, Martin
**Philosophy**

Being-in-the-world and thrownness: we are always already situated in a context we did not choose, and that situatedness is constitutive of understanding rather than a distortion of it. Relevant to CT's account of why AI models occupy the epistemic positions they do — their training distribution is a form of thrownness, not a neutral vantage point. Also relevant: Heidegger's analysis of breakdown as the moment when the ready-to-hand becomes present-at-hand — when a tool stops working smoothly and becomes an object of attention. CT sessions that surface model divergence are inducing a productive breakdown that makes the tool's structure visible.

- *Being and Time* (1927/1962) — the foundational text; being-in-the-world, thrownness, ready-to-hand/present-at-hand, care
- *"The Question Concerning Technology"* (1954, in *The Question Concerning Technology and Other Essays*) — technology as revealing/concealing; relevant to CT's claims about what multi-model structure discloses

### Laozi
**Philosophy (Daoist)**

Wu wei — acting in accordance with the nature of things rather than against them — is the ethical principle underlying CT's design heuristic of working with model crystallization dynamics rather than against them. You do not demand a model be what it is not; you find where its dynamics are already moving in the needed direction and work with that movement. Also relevant to the Ethics of Becoming's account of incremental, distributed change as the appropriate mode of engagement with complex systems.

- *Tao Te Ching* (c. 4th century BCE) — the primary text; chapters on wu wei, the nature of water, action through non-action

### Packer, Martin
**Educational Psychology / Qualitative Research Methodology**

Sociocultural hermeneutics: interpretation as a practice embedded in sociocultural context, not a neutral extraction of meaning from text. Relevant to CT's account of how model responses should be read — not as outputs from a neutral processing system but as productions shaped by training context, role assignment, and session structure. Also a direct methodological influence on CT's research approach: the session as a site of meaning-making, not a data-collection instrument.

- *The Science of Qualitative Research* (2011) — the primary methodological text; sociocultural embeddedness of interpretation; the most directly relevant work
- *Child Development: Understanding a Cultural Perspective* (2017) — development as participation in sociocultural practice; relevant to CT's Vygotskian commitments

### Palincsar, Annemarie Sullivan
**Educational Psychology / Learning Sciences**

Reciprocal teaching: a structured instructional method in which students rotate through the roles of predictor, questioner, clarifier, and summarizer in relation to a shared text. The direct source for CT's Reciprocity Tetra, which instantiates these roles across models rather than across students. Palincsar's research demonstrates that role rotation — making the epistemic moves of comprehension explicit and assignable — produces deeper understanding than passive reading. The CT translation: structured role assignment makes epistemic moves that models would not naturally take visible and actionable.

- *"Reciprocal Teaching of Comprehension-Fostering and Comprehension-Monitoring Activities"* (1984, Cognition and Instruction) — the founding empirical paper; the original role definitions
- Palincsar & Brown, *"Interactive Teaching to Promote Independent Learning from Text"* (1986, The Reading Teacher) — accessible treatment of the pedagogical rationale

### Piaget, Jean
**Developmental Psychology / Epistemology**

Equilibration: the process by which cognitive structures are reorganized in response to encounter with material that cannot be assimilated into existing schemas, producing accommodation and genuine structural change. Relevant to CT's account of what productive epistemic friction achieves — not just adding information but potentially forcing schema reorganization. Distinct from mere learning; equilibration is the mechanism of genuine conceptual development. Also relevant to the In-Between as a designed space where equilibration can occur rather than being short-circuited by premature resolution.

- *The Development of Thought: Equilibration of Cognitive Structures* (1977) — the most directly relevant text; equilibration as the engine of cognitive development
- *Biology and Knowledge* (1971) — the epistemological framework underlying the developmental psychology

### Searle, John
**Philosophy of Language / Philosophy of Mind**

Extended and systematized Austin's speech act theory. Searle's taxonomy of illocutionary acts (assertives, directives, commissives, expressives, declarations) and his account of sincerity conditions — the psychological states a speaker must be in for a speech act to be genuine — are directly relevant to CT's analysis of what AI models are doing when they produce responses. Can an AI model make a genuine assertion? The sincerity condition requires that the speaker believe what they assert. Whether that condition is met, and what it means if it is not, bears directly on how CT's output should be interpreted. Particularly sharp in the AI→AI dyad: if sincerity conditions are absent on both sides, what kind of exchange is occurring?

- *Speech Acts: An Essay in the Philosophy of Language* (1969) — the systematic treatment; illocutionary force, sincerity conditions, constitutive rules
- *"Minds, Brains, and Programs"* (1980, Behavioral and Brain Sciences) — the Chinese Room argument; directly relevant to questions of AI understanding and genuine vs. simulated cognitive states
- *Intentionality: An Essay in the Philosophy of Mind* (1983) — intentional states and their relationship to speech acts; relevant to the sincerity condition question

### Vygotsky, L.S.
**Developmental Psychology / Educational Psychology**

The zone of proximal development (ZPD) and the social origins of cognition: higher cognitive functions develop first between people and only subsequently become internalized. The more capable other — whether human or, in CT's extension, an AI model — scaffolds the learner into performance they could not achieve alone. CT's conductor position is structurally analogous to the more capable other in the ZPD: the human is not just using the models but is engaged in a scaffolded epistemic process in which the models enable performance the human could not achieve in solo inquiry. Also relevant: the claim that language is the primary tool of thought — that the structure of the dialogue shapes the structure of the thinking.

- *Mind in Society: The Development of Higher Psychological Processes* (1978) — the primary English-language text; ZPD, scaffolding, the social origins of cognition
- *Thought and Language* (1934/1986) — language as the medium of thought development; inner speech; the most theoretically rich text
- *"The Genesis of Higher Mental Functions"* (in *Mind in Society*) — the specific argument for the social-to-individual developmental trajectory

### Winnicott, D.W.
**Psychoanalysis / Developmental Psychology**

Transitional space: the space between inner psychic reality and external shared reality, first populated by the transitional object (the child's blanket or stuffed animal) and never outgrown — in adult life it reappears as the space of cultural experience, art, religion, creative work, and play. CT's In-Between Tetra is structurally analogous: a maintained territory between positions that enables movement without premature commitment. Winnicott's insistence that the capacity to sustain transitional space is an ongoing cognitive and relational achievement — not a stage to be completed — provides the theoretical grounding for why the In-Between is a designed condition that requires active maintenance, not a natural default.

- *Playing and Reality* (1971) — the primary text; transitional objects, transitional space, the location of cultural experience
- *The Maturational Processes and the Facilitating Environment* (1965) — the holding environment as the structural precondition for development; relevant to CT's session design as creating conditions for genuine inquiry
