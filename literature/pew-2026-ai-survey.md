## Headline findings

Adoption has roughly doubled in two years: about half of U.S. adults now report using AI chatbots, up from roughly a third in 2024, with about one-in-four using them daily. ChatGPT dominates the field — reported use by 44% of U.S. adults, more than double its 2023 share — with a steep drop-off after it. Claude sits at 6%, just above Character.ai at 3%.

## The ambient-skepticism finding

The finding most load-bearing for CT: more Americans predict AI will have a negative rather than positive impact on both society (40% vs. 16%) and on themselves personally (31% vs. 23%). This is not concentrated in older, less-exposed respondents — adults under 30 are *more* likely than older groups to expect a negative societal effect. The population is simultaneously heavy-adopting and skeptical; usage and distrust are co-occurring rather than trading off against each other.

Two-thirds of Americans say AI is advancing too quickly, versus 2% who say too slowly. This is the ambient posture CT's users arrive with — not naive enthusiasm, but active wariness alongside active use.

## Secondary findings of note

**Emotional use, already non-trivial.** About one-in-ten report using chatbots for emotional support, with a smaller share for companionship. This is the use case CT is most explicitly designed to handle with care, and the survey shows it is not a fringe behavior even at this early stage of adoption.

**Regulatory attitudes have inverted by party.** In 2024, Republicans were more skeptical of government AI regulation than Democrats (70% vs. 54% expressing skepticism). By 2026 the pattern reverses: Democrats now express more skepticism of regulation than Republicans (74% vs. 61%) — a substantial swing in two years, plausibly tracking the current administration's posture toward AI governance. Political context only; not a CT design input, but relevant background for anything CT publishes publicly that touches AI policy.

## CT relevance

Grounds the "ambient skepticism" framing referenced in passing during the June 20 thread: CT's design has to navigate users who are neither uncritical adopters nor AI-averse holdouts, but both heavy users and skeptics simultaneously. This complicates any pitch-deck assumption that adoption friction is the primary barrier — trust and adoption are already decoupled in the general population, which may mean CT's differentiation (multi-model triangulation, visible disagreement, explicit friction) has more purchase with this audience than a single-model competitor's fluency-first pitch would.

The emotional-support figure is a data point worth having on hand whenever CT's wellbeing-adjacent design choices (Threshold Tetra, RIP/RIFF pacing) need empirical grounding for why the emotional-use case is being taken seriously as a design constraint rather than an edge case.
