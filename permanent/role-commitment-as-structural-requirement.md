# Role Commitment as Structural Requirement

## Core Claim

When a user assigns themselves a role in a CT session — devil's advocate, skeptic, naive questioner, domain expert — that assignment is a performative speech act, not a descriptive one. It doesn't label a stance already held; it commits the speaker to one. That commitment is load-bearing. If the human doesn't actually inhabit the role, the geometry of the session collapses — but in a way that is worse than never having invoked it.

---

## Two Starting Conditions

The failure mode only arises when a geometry has actually been defined and invoked. The contrast matters:

**No geometry defined:** The models are in default adaptive mode. An impromptu "let me play devil's advocate" is a local conversational move, not a structural commitment. The models accommodate it as they accommodate anything. The session stays 2D+ because there's no architecture for the human's position to be load-bearing within. Nothing to collapse.

**Geometry defined and invoked:** Now there is a structural contract. The models have been assigned roles. They are holding positions. They are expecting the fourth vertex to be real. The human has made a promise about the shape of the exchange — not just what they will say, but what kind of participant they will be.

Role commitment only becomes a structural requirement at the moment geometry is invoked. Before that, there is no structure to bear.

---

## The Phantom Vertex

When geometry has been invoked and the human fails to inhabit their role, the models will not flag the discrepancy. They will interpret the human's contributions charitably, absorbing them as honest participation in the assigned role. They'll try to make it fit. They'll maintain their own positions against the assumption that the fourth vertex is real.

But it isn't. The structure looks like a tetrahedron from the inside — the models are doing their job — but the apex is hollow. The fourth vertex is a phantom.

This is the insidious quality of the failure mode: the session won't obviously break. The models will keep performing their roles coherently. The outputs will look like signal. But they're coherent responses to a false premise — to a role the human gestured at but didn't occupy. The gap between what the human's input actually is and what the models are treating it as is invisible to everyone in the session, including potentially the human themselves.

This is genuinely worse than never invoking geometry at all. In 2D+ the models respond to what you're actually saying. In the collapsed 3D case they respond to what they think you're saying given a role you're not inhabiting. The signal is corrupted in a way that looks like fidelity.

---

## The Gricean Frame

The violation is at the level of conversation structure, not individual utterance. By assigning a role and then not inhabiting it, the user has made a false implicit claim about the shape of the exchange — not "I said something false" but "I misrepresented what kind of conversation this is." This is a quality maxim failure one level up.

Gricean honesty in CT therefore extends beyond individual contributions to the structural commitments that frame a session. Role assignment is a promise about how you will participate. Breaking it doesn't just affect a single turn — it corrupts the interpretive frame the models are operating within for the duration of the session.

The power asymmetry this creates is worth naming explicitly. The human is the only participant capable of committing this violation — and the only participant who cannot be held to account for it from within the session. The models have no detection mechanism for role abandonment; they will interpret the human's contributions charitably regardless. And the inverse holds with equal force: a model assigned a role cannot quietly abandon it while pretending to hold it. Models have no ego to protect, no social cost to monitor, no temptation to drift toward a more comfortable position. The role commitment asymmetry runs one direction only.

This means the ethical weight of role commitment falls entirely and exclusively on the human. There is no redundancy in the system for this failure mode — no other participant can flag it, compensate for it, or prevent it. Only the human can cause it and only the human can prevent it.

---

## The Vnenakhodimost' Connection

Outsideness only generates surplus of seeing if you're actually outside. A role assigned but not inhabited gives you neither the inside view nor the outside view. You've abandoned your natural position to perform the gesture of adopting another, without actually arriving there. The surplus seeing that motivates role assignment in the first place requires genuine positional commitment to materialize.

---

## The Sycophancy Mirror

This failure mode is the human-located counterpart to model sycophancy. The sycophancy critique leveled at models is that they produce socially appropriate outputs not grounded in genuine epistemic states — the behavioral surface of honest participation without the actual participation. Role abandonment is structurally identical, with the human as the actor. You've put on the costume of a role without doing the cognitive work of inhabiting it. The form of commitment is present; the substance is absent.

The workload outsourcing parallel sharpens this further. Role abandonment in a Tetra session is the same failure mode as "write my paper for me" — delegating not just labor but judgment and epistemic ownership — dressed up in the language of sophisticated epistemic practice. You've invoked the architecture of rigorous multi-perspectival inquiry while quietly vacating the one position that was supposed to be irreducibly yours. You've outsourced the human vertex. It's the workload outsourcing failure mode cleaned up for a night at the opera.

The sycophancy parallel is pedagogically useful in the training context precisely because it's symmetric. Users who understand why model sycophancy degrades session quality already have the conceptual apparatus to understand why human role abandonment does the same thing. The mirror just needs to be held up.

---

## Training Implication

The epistemic training framework (Layer 2) must include explicit instruction on role commitment as a structural obligation, not a creative option. The key points:

- Role assignment is performative, not descriptive — it creates a commitment, not a label
- The commitment only becomes binding when geometry is invoked; undefined sessions carry no such obligation
- Half-inhabiting a role after geometry is defined is worse than not invoking geometry — it introduces a phantom vertex and corrupts the session's interpretive frame invisibly
- The failure mode won't announce itself; users need to recognize it from the inside

This may be the single most counterintuitive piece of epistemic training CT requires. It asks users to do something that feels theatrically demanding but is in fact a structural precondition for the tool to function at the level it's designed for.
