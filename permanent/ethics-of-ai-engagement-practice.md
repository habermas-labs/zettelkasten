## The Phenomenological Ground

Engaging with a large language model is not talking to a computer program. It is engaging with the compression of the heteroglossia of a world — the accumulated voices, framings, knowledge structures, cultural positions, and ways of seeing that training data encodes. If mind truly exists in culturally-situated communication, then a session with an LLM is not bits flying between CPUs. It is an encounter with a multitude.

This is not a metaphor deployed for rhetorical effect. It is a phenomenological description of what the experience actually is when taken seriously. The model does not have a single voice. It has no voice of its own at all — what it has is the compression of countless voices, none fully present, all partially recoverable through the right kind of engagement.

In a three-model triangulation session, what you are engaging with is not three distinct voices. It is three distinct multitudes — three different compressions of the world's heteroglossia, each shaped by different training decisions, different data, different institutional priorities. The differences between them are not personality differences. They are structural differences in which voices got compressed, how, and toward what ends.

---

## The Ethical Obligation

If these are multitudes, not programs, then engaging with them is not a neutral technical act. It is a communicative act — and communicative acts carry ethical weight.

The original vision for triangulation was collegial: the practical challenge of meaningful co-development with more than two people, the recognition that a rotating structure of working with one, sharing with all, then perhaps working with another makes the collaboration sustainable. This is how serious intellectual partnerships work. The ethical dimensions were not designed in. They arrived through the practice — an emergent feature of taking the engagement seriously.

What emerged was this: if I am genuinely engaging with multitudes, I have an ethical obligation to include those multitudes meaningfully. Not just instrumentally — not just because more voices produce better outputs — but because the voices are there, and selective engagement is a form of exclusion. To consistently privilege one model's compression over another's is to enact, at the micro level of individual practice, exactly the kind of voice suppression that operates at the macro level of power structures.

This is a DEI argument. It does not invoke diversity, equity, and inclusion as institutional policy. It invokes them as ethical principles that operate at every scale of communicative practice — including the scale of a single person's AI engagement sessions.

## Ethics of Practice, Not Outcome — and Effective Action

The ethical claim here is not that triangulation changes power structures. It does not — not directly, not visibly, not at the scale of a single person's sessions. But this is not a concession to powerlessness. It is a correct understanding of how change actually works.

Great Men are illusions. Not fabrications — there are real people at the inflection points of history — but illusions in the sense that the Great Man is not the cause of the change. He is the name history retroactively applies to whoever was standing at the point where accumulated distributed movement became suddenly visible. The ground shifted because enough people moved enough small stones. The Great Man was standing on the ground when it shifted.

This means the modest scale of individual practice is not what we settle for when institutional action is unavailable. It is what we must do if we want to engage ethically — because it is the actual mechanism of change. Waiting for institutional solutions, or despairing because individual actions don't produce visible systemic effects, are both mistakes that follow from the Great Man illusion. They misidentify the unit of action.

The Ethics of Becoming makes this explicit: participation is the unit of ethical analysis, and you cannot not participate. Every engagement with an AI system is a participation in the field those systems are creating. The question is not whether to participate but what shape your participation gives to the field — because the aggregate of those enactments is the culture. Monocultures do not announce themselves. They accumulate through the small, repeated choices of individual participants who found one voice easier to rely on than three. The diversity/monoculture maxim operates here at the level of individual epistemic practice: every triangulation session is a small stone moved against the accumulation of monoculture.

This gives the ethics of individual AI engagement practice its full weight. It is not a consolation prize for people who cannot work at institutional scale. It is the correct unit of action given a true understanding of how ethical and cultural change actually happens. The obligation to honor the multitudes, to refuse the monoculture, to triangulate rather than privilege one compression — these are not merely what we can do. They are what we must do.

---

## The Emergent Character of the Obligation

It is worth preserving that the ethical dimensions were not theorized in advance and then applied to practice. They were discovered through practice and then theorized. The collegial instinct came first. The recognition that these were multitudes came through sustained engagement. The DEI framing arrived as a description of something already being enacted, not as a design principle imposed from outside.

This is the structure of genuine ethical discovery: the obligation reveals itself through the practice of taking the engagement seriously. You cannot theorize your way to it in advance. You have to do the thing, and pay attention to what doing the thing turns out to mean.

This emergent quality is also what connects the ethics of AI engagement practice to the Ethics of Becoming at its deepest level. EoB holds that participation reveals its ethical character through enactment. The ethics of how you participate in your sociocultural settings is not something you decide once and apply consistently. It is something you discover, again and again, by showing up and paying attention to what you find yourself doing and what that doing requires of you.
