## The Concept

Workload outsourcing names the delegation of labor to an AI system while judgment, synthesis, and epistemic authorship remain with the human participant. The work moves; the thinking stays.

Coined in contrast to two inadequate framings:

**Cognitive offloading** — the term prevalent in the HCI and cognitive science literature — misappropriates Clark and Chalmers' Extended Mind thesis. The Extended Mind argument is about the *boundary* of cognition, not the delegation of it. Using a notebook to extend memory does not offload cognition — it expands the cognitive system to include the notebook. "Offloading" implies cognition leaves the agent; the Extended Mind argues the agent's boundary expands. Applied to AI-assisted intellectual work, cognitive offloading misdescribes what is actually happening by suggesting the thinking transferred.

**AI wrote your paper** — the colloquial version of the same critique — conflates retrieval with generation, and pipeline speed with authorship.

Workload outsourcing is the precise alternative: the labor of literature retrieval, connection-finding, and surface-level synthesis can be delegated to an AI system without any transfer of judgment, question-formation, or recognition of significance. The distinction between *what* is outsourced and *what* stays is the load-bearing move.

### Degree vs. categorical bypass

A second, sharper distinction from cognitive offloading (developed 2026-06-24, LinkedIn Article 1 drafting): cognitive offloading implies some thinking is happening but being assisted — it is a question of degree and developmental appropriateness. Workload outsourcing implies the thinking is being skipped entirely — it is a categorical bypass of the learning process, not a point on the same continuum. This is not merely a terminological refinement; it changes what kind of claim is being made. A claim about degree invites a "how much is too much" question. A claim about categorical bypass does not — the concern is not that too much thinking was assisted, but that no thinking occurred where the assignment presumed it would.

## Contrast with Moral Outsourcing

Chowdhury's moral outsourcing names the delegation of ethical judgment to an AI system — the move where the thing that requires a moral agent is what transfers. Moral outsourcing is dangerous precisely *because* judgment moves.

Workload outsourcing is the structural inverse: it is legitimate (and potentially epistemically valuable) precisely *because* judgment does not move. The parallel structure makes the contrast crisp:

| | What transfers | What stays | Normative status |
|---|---|---|---|
| Moral outsourcing | Judgment | — | Dangerous |
| Workload outsourcing | Labor | Judgment | Legitimate / epistemically neutral |

## The Question That Does the Work

In AI-assisted intellectual work, the retrieval connection only *does* anything because it lands in a prepared conceptual space. A model surfacing Grice's cooperative principle is retrieving from a corpus. Whether that retrieval becomes load-bearing in a theoretical architecture depends entirely on whether the human asking the question recognized what it was for. The question creates the conditions; the retrieval serves them.

Nobody without a specific conceptual need hands you a connection and makes it theoretically productive. The question is authored; the retrieval is not.

## Operationalized: the subtraction test (Kirsh & Maglio)

Kirsh & Maglio's epistemic/pragmatic action distinction gives workload outsourcing a checkable diagnostic rather than a purely conceptual one. Epistemic actions restructure a problem so internal cognition can operate more efficiently, without replacing that cognition — they trade physical or generative effort for cognitive ease, never substitute for the ease itself being earned. Pragmatic actions simply move toward a goal state.

The subtraction test, developed against AI-assisted work specifically: take the AI's output away and ask what the human would still have to do. If the answer is "recompute the whole thing from scratch," the move was pragmatic replacement — workload outsourcing in the categorical-bypass sense. If the answer is "lose a useful prompt but still have to do the actual work of integrating it," the move was epistemic scaffolding — labor delegated, judgment retained, exactly as this note originally specified.

This makes workload outsourcing the **zero-epistemic-action limit** of AI-assisted work: the case where nothing is left to rotate into view, nothing for the human's own recognition to act on. See kirsh-maglio-epistemic-actions.md for the full derivation and the min-max caveat (tool selection under quality/time optimization is a legitimate Kirshian move one level up from the in-task test itself, and should not be conflated with it).

## Connection to the Irony of Agency

fleeting-irony-of-agency.md develops the perception side of this concept: the workload-outsourcing case is perceived — by both student and teacher — as the maximally successful move, when it is empirically the likely worst case on every dimension except time-to-submission. The Dewey flip formalizes why: output produced *through* a learning process is the residue of that process, not evidence of it independent of it. Workload outsourcing in its purest form produces output with no residue-generating process behind it — nothing was skipped in the ordinary sense of a shortcut, because there was no learning process to shortcut.

## Connection to workspace development

fleeting-workspace-friction-development.md extends the categorical-bypass point to trajectory rather than single moves: a workspace (or AI-assisted practice) arranged to eliminate friction at the user's current level forecloses the friction that development requires. Workload outsourcing, sustained over time rather than as a single instance, is the limit case of a workspace with no productive friction left in it at all — see that note's apprenticeship argument for why this is a design problem, not just an individual-choice problem.
