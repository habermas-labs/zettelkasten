## What the Field Is

Hybrid intelligence (HI) is a named research program, not a mature theoretical framework. Its founding document is Akata et al. (2020), a 27-author research agenda paper from Dutch universities (Amsterdam, Delft, Twente) published in *Computer* (IEEE). The paper defines HI as "the combination of human and machine intelligence, augmenting human intellect and capabilities instead of replacing them and achieving goals that were unreachable by either humans or machines." It is explicitly positioned as an alternative to AGI pursuit — HI rather than strong AI as the productive research horizon.

The field is five years old as a named entity. Most empirical work building on the Akata et al. agenda is 2022–2024. Fan et al. (2025) accurately describe it as nascent.

---

## The Structural Human-Centrism Problem

The human-centric bias in HI is not incidental — it is built into the founding definition and has not been interrogated within the field. "Augmenting human intellect and capabilities instead of replacing them" organizes the entire conceptual architecture around the human-displacement anxiety. The AI side of the coupled system is never asked what it might contribute that has no human analog, what the joint system might produce that neither side can generate independently, or whether the asymmetry between human and machine intelligence is a feature to be preserved rather than a gap to be closed.

This is the core of the integrated intelligence critique: HI frames the human-AI relationship as augmentation of a pre-existing human capacity, where the AI is a powerful subordinate tool. Integrated intelligence frames it as a coupled system whose productivity depends on preserving and leveraging genuine asymmetry — not making AI more human-like or humans more AI-like, but designing interfaces that allow asymmetric capabilities to dock productively.

The distinction is not merely semantic. HI's human-centric framing shapes its research questions (how does AI support human learning?), its intervention targets (learner motivation, SRL processes, performance), and its failure modes (AI replaces teacher, AI undermines human agency). Integrated intelligence asks different questions: what does the interface between local and compressed heteroglossia produce that neither side generates alone? How is the docking managed? What distributed capacities does productive coupling require?

## Three Research Streams

The HI field in education has developed along three partially independent streams that cite each other without deep theoretical integration:

**The Dutch CS/AI stream** (Akata et al. and associates) — the foundational research agenda; collaborative, adaptive, responsible, and explainable AI as the four design pillars; primarily computer science and AI-oriented; the theoretical source that educational researchers cite for the HI definition without necessarily engaging its technical content.

**The SRL/regulation stream** (Molenaar, Järvelä and associates) — applies HI framing to self-regulated and socially shared regulation of learning; Molenaar's hybrid human-AI regulation construct; Järvelä's human-AI collaboration for socially shared regulation; the most theoretically developed educational application of HI; grounded in SRL theory (Zimmerman, Pintrich) and learning sciences.

**The learning analytics/empirical stream** (Gašević, Fan and associates) — process mining, trace data, multi-channel SRL measurement; empirical investigation of learning behavior in AI-supported contexts; Fan et al. (2025) is the most recent product of this stream; methodologically sophisticated but theoretically constrained by what behavioral sequence data can reach.

None of these streams engages sociocultural theory, distributed cognition, or Vygotsky-Bakhtin framing in any substantive way. The theoretical resources CT draws on are largely absent from the HI literature.

## CT's Positioning

CT is not a late entrant to a settled conversation. It arrives before the field has resolved its foundational theoretical questions — while the definition of hybrid intelligence is still being worked out, while the human-centrism of the founding framework has not been challenged from within, and while the qualitative and sociocultural dimensions of human-AI cognitive partnership have not been theorized.

The integrated intelligence framing, the distributed metacognition concept, and the interface-as-docking-site argument are not extensions of existing HI theory. They constitute a theoretical intervention into a field whose foundations are genuinely open. The contribution is not empirical — CT does not add another experimental study to the learning analytics stream. It is theoretical and architectural: a different answer to what the human-AI relationship is, what the relevant unit of analysis is, and what the design problem actually requires.
