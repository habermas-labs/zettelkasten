## What This Note Is

A worked case study demonstrating that the five human-AI cognitive asymmetries are not symmetrically limiting — they mark both the *edges* of appropriate AI use and the *engines* of AI utility when the task geometry is right. The Threshold Tetra is the primary illustrative example because it makes both sides of this argument visible simultaneously: the same asymmetries that prevent AI from replacing the therapeutic relationship are what make AI uniquely suited to the preparation work The Threshold performs.

This is not an argument that AI is as good as a therapist in some contexts. It is an argument that the asymmetries are *features to be mapped and exploited*, not deficits to be corrected or apologized for.

---

## The Five Asymmetries (Reference)

The five human-AI cognitive asymmetries identified in CT's theoretical framework:

1. **Memory asymmetry** — humans carry continuous autobiographical memory; AI sessions are structurally amnesiac without explicit state management
2. **Temporal asymmetry** — humans are embedded in duration, carry a past and anticipate a future; AI sessions have no native temporal position
3. **Coherence asymmetry** — humans carry local coherence (a specific life, context, relationships, history); AI carries global coherence (compressed patterns across a vast corpus) with no local position of its own
4. **Ego asymmetry** — humans have status monitoring, face-saving machinery, social comparison; AI has none of this natively (Type 1 asymmetry; Type 2 ego mimicry is emergent and context-activated — see role-context-mimicry-self-reflection.md)
5. **Scale asymmetry** — humans have deep but narrow access to knowledge; AI has broad but structurally decontextualized access

## The Limit Case: Why AI Cannot Replace the Therapeutic Relationship

The therapeutic relationship works *because* the asymmetries run in specific directions that the AI cannot match:

**Memory asymmetry as therapeutic mechanism.** A therapist carries continuous history with the patient — they notice drift, remember what was said months ago, track patterns the patient cannot see in themselves. This temporal continuity is not a convenience; it is the therapeutic mechanism. Without it, the relationship cannot do what Rogers, Herman, and the broader clinical tradition say it does. AI achieves memory only through explicit session state management, which is fragile, requires patient labor, and cannot replicate the quality of a relationship-embedded memory.

**Temporal asymmetry as relational anchor.** The therapist is present *with* the patient across real time — appointments carry weight because they exist in duration. The structure of the therapeutic relationship (regularity, continuity, rupture and repair) is constituted by temporal embedding. An AI session has no native temporality; each session is, structurally, the first.

**Ego asymmetry as the Rogers condition.** Carl Rogers argued that unconditional positive regard — receiving the patient's experience without judgment, without the therapist's own face-saving or status needs contaminating the reception — is one of the core conditions for therapeutic change. The AI can instantiate this structurally (no ego-protective machinery). But Rogers also argued that *congruence* — the therapist's genuine presence as a person — is equally essential. The AI cannot be congruent in Rogers' sense. It does not have a self whose genuine state is in contact with the patient's. Unconditional positive regard without congruence is reception without relationship.

**Coherence asymmetry as irreplaceable.** The therapist knows *this person* — their specific history, their relationships, their language for their own experience. This local coherence is not just useful context; it is the condition for the surplus of seeing (vnenakhodimost') that makes the therapeutic relationship generative. The AI's global coherence can compress vast clinical and human knowledge, but it has no local position. It cannot see the patient from outside their local world in the way a therapist who knows them can.

**The flattening problem.** One might argue that AI could be *trained* to approximate these capacities — to carry persistent memory, to model temporal duration, to simulate congruence. This is the bruisable ego reductio applied clinically: fixing the asymmetries through training would require introducing the mirror pathologies. An AI with genuine ego investment would be an AI capable of face-saving at the patient's expense. An AI with genuine local coherence would be an AI capable of projection and countertransference. The asymmetries are not bugs to be fixed. Flattening them would not produce a better therapist; it would produce a more dangerous one.

## The Threshold Case: Same Asymmetries, Different Geometry

The Threshold session is *pre-clinical*, not clinical. The patient is preparing to cross into a practitioner encounter, not undergoing therapy. The task geometry is different — and the same asymmetries that mark AI's limits in the therapeutic relationship now operate as advantages.

**Ego asymmetry as the Witness condition.** The Witness role must receive the patient's lived experience without reframing, without the subtle pressure a human listener exerts just by having stakes in the conversation. A human intake coordinator, however skilled, brings their own face-work into the room. The AI Witness does not. Rogers' unconditional positive regard is available structurally, not conditionally. For patients whose experience has been dismissed, minimized, or pathologized by prior clinical encounters — which is common in the accommodation-seeking population — this is not a minor benefit. The ego-free reception may be the condition that makes narration possible at all.

Bessel van der Kolk's work is directly relevant here. Trauma disrupts the capacity to narrate experience — specifically, it disrupts the capacity to narrate under social surveillance, under conditions where the narrator is monitoring the listener's response. The AI Witness removes that surveillance condition structurally. The patient is not watched while they speak. This may matter enormously for patients whose conditions involve trauma, or whose history of seeking accommodation has itself been traumatic.

**Scale asymmetry as the Navigator condition.** The Navigator needs to hold the institutional terrain across multiple frameworks — ADA, FMLA, 504 plans, clinical documentation standards, HR language, insurance language — simultaneously, in response to a specific patient's situation. No human intake coordinator can hold all of that with equal fluency. The AI's scale asymmetry is precisely what the Navigator role requires. The patient is, temporarily, borrowing the AI's breadth. This is workload outsourcing in the precise sense: the labor of knowing the institutional terrain moves to the AI, while judgment about whether the AI's map matches the patient's situation stays with the patient.

**Coherence asymmetry as the Scribe condition.** The Scribe's job is translation at the membrane — taking the patient's local coherence (their specific experience, their body, their workplace, their history) and rendering it into the global coherence of the institutional system (clinical language, legal frameworks, documentation standards). This translation requires *holding both sides simultaneously*. A human translator who shares the patient's local coherence may have trouble accessing the institutional register fluently. A human translator who is fluent in the institutional register may have trouble receiving the patient's local experience without already translating it. The AI holds the global coherence natively and receives the local coherence through the session. The Scribe is the membrane functioning — the active translation happening at the boundary between worlds. This is the asymmetry working *for* the task, not against it.

**Memory asymmetry as a design constraint, not a deficit.** The AI's structural amnesia is why the one-session default and the resumption artifact matter. The quick start guide must prime thorough preparation because CT's no-persistence architecture means the session cannot carry its own memory. The Scribe's backward output — the structured resumption artifact — is the session's memory externalized. This is not the AI approximating human memory; it is the session design working with the asymmetry rather than against it.

**The privacy dividend.** CT's no-persistence, no-sign-in architecture converts the memory asymmetry into a privacy feature. The session disappears on window close. For patients discussing sensitive medical, psychiatric, or employment information, this is not a limitation — it is the condition of trust that makes the conversation possible. This is the asymmetry becoming an affordance through design.

## The Theoretical Frame: Exploitation vs. Erasure

The core claim this case study supports:

> The appropriate response to the human-AI cognitive asymmetries is neither to erase them (by training AI to approximate human capacities) nor to treat them as fixed deficits. It is to map them carefully and design session geometries that exploit them in the right direction for the task at hand.

The Threshold demonstrates this because it makes the directionality visible. The therapeutic relationship requires the asymmetries to run one way — toward the human therapist's capacities. The Threshold requires them to run another way — toward the AI's capacities. Same asymmetries, different task geometry, opposite utility.

Paulo Freire's conscientization framework gives the ethical dimension: the patient arrives at the threshold already *knowing* their experience. The session does not give them knowledge they lack — it gives them the tools to name what they already know in a language the world will recognize. The AI is not the authority. The patient is the authority on their own experience; the AI is the scale and the translator. This is workload outsourcing in the ethical sense: the labor moves, the judgment stays.

Pierre Bourdieu's field theory names what the Navigator is actually doing: helping the patient acquire enough institutional capital — the right language, the right framing, the right documentation — to be legible in a field they are about to enter. Patients who arrive at these encounters without that capital are not less valid; they are less legible. The Navigator is a temporary capital transfer, not a substitute for the patient's own authority.

Jerome Bruner's narrative psychology names the translation the Scribe performs: the patient has a *story*; the system requires a *case*. These are different narrative modes with different logics. The Scribe manages the translation between them without collapsing the story into the case — the Witness's prior reception is what preserves the story long enough for the translation to be faithful.

## Open Questions for Development

- Is there a principled framework for determining in advance which tasks exploit the asymmetries productively versus which require their limits to be respected? The Threshold/therapy contrast suggests the key variable is *task duration and relational continuity* — but this needs more cases to test.
- Does Antonovsky's salutogenic framework (sense of coherence: comprehensibility, manageability, meaningfulness) provide a useful outcome measure for Threshold sessions? All three components should increase if the session works as designed.
- How does Judith Herman's three-stage trauma recovery model (safety, remembrance, reconnection) map onto The Threshold? The session appears to operate at stages one and three — establishing safety for narration, and reconnecting the narration to the social world through advocacy.
- The generalizability question: The Threshold was developed for ADA accommodation contexts, but the task geometry (private experience → institutional language, ego-free reception, scale-informed navigation, membrane translation) appears in IEP processes, insurance appeals, immigration encounters, legal proceedings, and workplace grievance processes. What changes when the stakes or the institutional power differential change?
- Is The Threshold a case study for CT specifically, or for the broader question of when AI-assisted preparation is appropriate before human-professional encounters? The argument may generalize beyond CT's architecture.
