## Core Distinction

Most software training is operational: here is the button, here is what it does, here is the workflow. Crosstalk Lab requires that layer but it is the smaller layer.

The larger layer is epistemic: here is how to think differently when you are inside this tool. Here is what you are trying to do with your mind, not just your hands.

CT is not a productivity tool. It is a sense-making tool. The goal is not to get tasks done faster — it is to think better. Because of that, the user needs training not just in how to operate it, but in how to think with it. How to approach it. Thought and language patterns to play with.

---

## Three-Layer Structure

### Layer 1 — Operational
What you'd expect: interface walkthrough, key concepts, modes, Tetras, how to configure and run a session. Necessary but not sufficient. Documented primarily through the Quick Start Guide and devstack reference.

### Layer 2 — Epistemic
How to read a session in progress. How to recognize when friction is productive versus circular. How to sense when a model is drifting from its role. When to intervene and when to let tension develop. How to conduct toward resolution versus how to sustain productive incompletion.

**Hedra gestures** live here — informal pattern language for moves a conductor makes in the moment before they become formalized presets. A Hedra gesture is an improvisational facing assignment: noticing that a response opened something, deciding to rotate a facing, acting on that intuition. This is a learned skill, not a feature that can be documented with a screenshot.

The epistemic layer corresponds to the move from 1D to 2D and beyond on the dimensionality ladder — the point at which the human conductor becomes structurally load-bearing rather than externally observing.

### Layer 3 — Reflective
What you do with what a session produces. How to exit a session well. How to carry insights forward into the vault. How session artifacts (transcript, tension map, summary) serve different reflective functions. The relationship between CT sessions and Zettelkasten note development.

Mode exit scaffolding (CT-047) is the application-layer instantiation of this layer.

## Hedra Gestures as Training Concept

Hedra gestures are the informal version of Hedra presets — conductor moves that assign a facing without formalizing it as a named configuration. Examples from live sessions:

- Dropping a model's response into another model's prompt with no framing, observing what facing the receiving model adopts by default
- Explicitly rotating a facing mid-session: "look at this from the angle of what ChatGPT just said"
- Using the Include mechanic to create implicit perspective pressure without a direct instruction

Hedra gestures are trainable. They represent the point where operational fluency tips into epistemic fluency — the conductor stops operating the tool and starts thinking with it.

The training guide should include a Hedra gestures section with annotated examples from real sessions, not hypothetical ones. The water-is-wet and The In-Between sessions are early candidates.

---

## The Training Guide as CT Artifact

The training guide itself should be developed through CT sessions rather than written top-down. Using CT to develop the guide for CT is consistent with the project's recursive methodology — CT's own feature design is its primary test bed, and the training guide is an extension of that principle into documentation.

This also means the guide will have genuine session transcripts as examples rather than constructed scenarios, preserving provenance and epistemic authenticity.
