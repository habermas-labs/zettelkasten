## Who Rumman Chowdhury Is

Rumman Chowdhury is CEO and co-founder of Humane Intelligence, a nonprofit advancing community-driven AI auditing and evaluation. She holds a PhD in political science and a master's in quantitative social science methods from Columbia — a social scientist who became a technologist, bringing systems-level thinking and structural inequality analysis to applied AI ethics. Previously she directed the ML Ethics, Transparency, and Accountability team at Twitter. She has testified to Congress, advised the Biden administration as the first U.S. Science Envoy for AI, and serves on governance bodies including Oxford's Commission on AI and Governance and the UK Center for Data Ethics and Innovation. Named to TIME's 100 Most Influential People in AI.

The biographical detail that matters here: the same interdisciplinary movement from social science to technology that shapes the CT project, made from a different starting point and at a different scale.

## The Distributed Mechanism Connection

The deepest resonance is structural. As argued in the ethics of individual AI engagement practice note, Great Men are illusions — change happens through the accumulated distributed movement of many people making many small decisions, and the "Great Man" is the name history applies to whoever stood at the inflection point when the movement became visible.

Chowdhury's red-teaming exercises and bias bounty challenges are the institutional-scale coordination of exactly this distributed mechanism. Hack the Future — the largest generative AI red-teaming collaboration, with eight major AI companies sharing LLMs for 4,000 people to evaluate — is not a Great Man intervention. It is a structured coordination of distributed judgment. She is creating conditions under which individual stone-movers can act directionally without centralizing into a single authoritative voice.

CT operates at the complementary scale: the individual practice level, where a single conductor's engagement with AI systems becomes more rigorous, more intentional, and more genuinely multi-voiced. The individual practice level and the coordination level are not hierarchically related. They are structurally necessary to each other. You cannot coordinate what isn't happening, and what happens at the individual level is shaped by whether the tools available to individual practitioners encourage monoculture or diversity.

## Community-Driven Auditing as Parallel to Triangulation

Chowdhury's core methodological commitment is community-driven evaluation — the refusal of the expert-panel model in which a small number of credentialed authorities determine what AI systems are doing and what harms they produce. Instead, evaluation is distributed across a community of practitioners who bring diverse vantage points, lived experiences, and domain knowledge that no expert panel could aggregate.

This is structurally parallel to what triangulation does at the session level. CT refuses the single-authoritative-output model — the assumption that one model's response is the response, that the first answer you receive is the answer worth keeping. Instead it distributes the evaluative function across multiple compressions of the world's heteroglossia, treating the convergences and divergences between them as the primary epistemic product rather than any single output.

Both approaches rest on the same underlying epistemological commitment: that distributed judgment is more reliable than centralized judgment, that the surplus of seeing generated by multiple external positions cannot be recovered by any single position however expert, and that the goal of evaluation is not to arrive at the authoritative answer but to map the shape of the question more completely.

## The Mediation Layer Argument

Chowdhury's most pointed ethical concern is with unmediated AI use — any deployment that places AI output directly in the path of consequential decisions affecting human lives without meaningful human input and the ability to make an informed judgment about the model. The mediation is not just a procedural safeguard. It is the site where human judgment, values, and accountability enter the system.

CT is a mediation layer — but a structured one. It doesn't merely insert a human between AI output and decision. It creates conditions under which the human's engagement with AI output is epistemically productive: the conductor sees multiple responses simultaneously, observes convergence and divergence, makes routing decisions that reflect deliberate epistemic strategy, and accumulates a session record that preserves the structure of the inquiry rather than just its conclusions.

This is what distinguishes CT from simply "asking multiple AIs." The epistemic infrastructure — roles, modes, directed routing, provenance preservation, the input layer as surface of user agency — transforms the mediation from nominal to substantive. The human is not just in the loop. The human is conducting the loop.

## Power Distribution in Communication

Chowdhury's concern with power operates at the institutional level: who controls AI development, who determines evaluation criteria, who bears the consequences of AI decisions, whose communities serve as test grounds for unproven technology. These are structural questions about the concentration of AI's benefits and harms.

The CT framework's concern with power operates at the communicative level: whose voices get honored in an AI engagement session, which compression of the world's heteroglossia gets privileged, what it means to enact inclusion at the scale of individual practice. These are not the same question. But they share an underlying structure — both are asking about whose voices count, at what scale, and under what conditions.

The conjunction is important. Institutional power structures are not changed by individual practice alone. But individual practice is not independent of institutional power structures either. The tools available to individual practitioners — whether they encourage monoculture or diversity, whether they make the distributed mechanism legible or invisible — are shaped by institutional decisions. And the aggregate of individual practices shapes the culture that institutional structures must eventually respond to.

## Where They Diverge — and Why That Matters

Chowdhury is working on AI systems as objects of evaluation: what are these systems doing, what harms do they produce, how can those harms be identified and mitigated. CT is working on AI engagement as a practice: how should individuals engage with AI systems in order to think more rigorously, honor more voices, and resist the epistemic monoculture that single-model reliance produces.

These are adjacent but distinct questions. She is asking about the systems. CT is asking about the engagement. The distinction matters for a potential collaboration: CT is not a tool for auditing AI systems in Chowdhury's sense — it is a tool for conducting the kind of rigorous, multi-voiced engagement that her auditing framework assumes should be possible but does not itself provide at the individual practice level.

There may be a genuine gap here that CT addresses: between the institutional evaluation work Humane Intelligence does and the individual practitioner who wants to engage with AI more ethically and rigorously. CT could be the practice-level complement to institutional auditing — the tool that makes the distributed mechanism's individual nodes more capable.

## The Biographical Resonance

Both projects emerge from the conjunction of social science training and AI engagement. Chowdhury's political science and quantitative social science background gives her a structural-inequality lens that pure technologists lack. The CT project's grounding in Bakhtin, Vygotsky, research psychology, and the Ethics of Becoming gives it a communicative-practice and developmental lens that pure technologists equally lack.

Neither project is a technology project with ethics bolted on. Both are projects in which the ethical and theoretical framework is constitutive of the tool or method — the reason it is built the way it is, not a post-hoc justification for decisions made on other grounds.

This is not incidental to a potential collaboration. It is the basis of the conversation.
