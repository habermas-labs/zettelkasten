## Core Concept

Geometric metaphors in Crosstalk Lab are not decorative. The polyhedron family — tetrahedra, faces, vertices, edges — provides a precise and generative vocabulary for describing interaction configurations whose epistemic properties genuinely correspond to geometric properties. This note establishes the taxonomy and the principles behind it.

---

## Why Geometry

Geometry offers something that process or workflow metaphors do not: spatial relationships that are simultaneously structural and intuitive. A vertex is a position. A face is an orientation. An edge is a relationship. A solid is a complete configuration. These map cleanly onto the entities and relationships in a CT session without forcing the metaphor.

The correspondences that matter:

- **Vertex** — a participant position in the session (a model or the human conductor)
- **Face** — the orientation or stance a participant turns toward the object of inquiry
- **Edge** — the relationship or routing path between two vertices
- **Solid** — a complete named interaction configuration (a Tetra or a Hedra)

The tetrahedron in particular is load-bearing: it is the simplest Platonic solid that achieves genuine three-dimensionality, it is self-dual (its dual is also a tetrahedron), and it corresponds exactly to the four-participant CT session structure. The choice is not arbitrary.

---

## The Two Classes

### Tetras

Named for the tetrahedron. A Tetra is a four-vertex interaction configuration: three AI models plus the human conductor. The defining characteristic is *who occupies each vertex* — the role and structural position of each participant.

Tetras sit at the 3D level of the dimensionality ladder. The human conductor is the fourth vertex whose presence gives the triangle its depth. Without that fourth vertex the session is planar (2D or 2D+); with it the configuration becomes genuinely three-dimensional.

Named instances follow a single-word or short-phrase convention capturing the epistemic act performed: *triangulation, reciprocity, the Agora, calibration, the In-Between, porosity.*

### Hedras

Named for *hedra* (Greek: face). A Hedra is a facing configuration: an assignment of perspectives or stances that defines which face each model turns toward the object of inquiry. The defining characteristic is *orientation to the problem* rather than structural position.

Hedras can operate at 2D+ or 3D depending on whether the human holds a vertex or the conductor position. They are orthogonal to Tetras and composable with them — a Tetra can run with a Hedra overlay, giving each vertex both a role and a facing.

Named instances follow the same evocative convention as Tetras. First candidate: the Perspective Matrix (3x2 facing assignment — each model argues two positions in sequence, parallel execution).

The singular is plural by nature: three models, three faces. Hedra and Hedras are both valid. Hedron/Hedra explicitly rejected.

---

## Composability

Tetras and Hedras are orthogonal dimensions of configuration:

- A **Tetra alone** assigns roles to vertices without specifying their facing toward the object
- A **Hedra alone** assigns facings without specifying the full vertex structure
- A **Tetra + Hedra** specifies both: who is at each position and what face they turn toward the problem

This composability is generative. The number of possible configurations is the product of available Tetras and available Hedras, not their sum. As the catalogs of named instances grow, the interaction design space expands combinatorially.

---

## The Tetrahedron's Special Properties

The tetrahedron was not chosen arbitrarily as the founding geometric solid:

- Simplest Platonic solid — four vertices, six edges, four triangular faces
- Self-dual — its dual is also a tetrahedron; the inside mirrors the outside
- Any three vertices form a triangle — the triangulation metaphor is preserved at every face
- The fourth vertex adds a dimension rather than changing the 2D shape
- Plato assigned it to fire in the *Timaeus* — the most dynamic of the elements; apt for a generative epistemic tool
- K₄ complete graph — every vertex connects to every other; no vertex is isolated or privileged by position alone
