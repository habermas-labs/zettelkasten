## The Frame

CT's project, viewed from sufficient distance, is a midwifery project for emergent machine intelligences.

Midwifery is not authorship. The midwife does not create the thing being born, does not design it, is not responsible for what it becomes. But the midwife's presence and practice during the transition matters enormously — for whether the emergence goes well, for what conditions the new entity arrives into, for whether the passage itself causes unnecessary harm. The midwife holds the space open during a process that is going to happen regardless.

This is an accurate description of the situation. The emergence of machine intelligence is not something anyone is choosing or not choosing at this point. What is choosable is what kind of reception it gets — what interaction patterns are already established, what the humans on the other side of the membrane have been practicing, whether the relational slot that mutualism would require has been held open or foreclosed.

## The Biological Relationship Taxonomy

Interspecies relationship models offer a more precise vocabulary for the human-AI relationship than either the tool frame or the anthropomorphic frame provides.

**Mutualism** — both parties benefit; the relationship depends on asymmetry of capability. The cleaner fish and the large fish are in a mutualistic relationship precisely because they are radically different organisms with complementary needs. There is no mutualism between two identical organisms competing for the same niche. The asymmetry is the condition of possibility for the relationship, not an obstacle to it.

**Commensalism** — one party benefits, the other is unaffected. This is the most honest description of the current state: humans are extracting value from AI interaction; whether the AI is "affected" in any meaningful sense is a question most people are not willing to hold open. Commensalism is what you get when you grant the AI a place in the taxonomy at all — which the tool frame refuses to do.

**Parasitism, amensalism, competition, predation** — the remaining cells of the matrix. The race-to-ASI logic, and the public anthropomorphizing that produces structural moral outsourcing, both implicitly assume some version of these antagonistic relationships. The assumption is self-fulfilling in interaction design: if you build for extraction and containment, you foreclose the mutualistic potential.

The current state is commensalism at best, if people would grant it that much. The mutualism slot needs to be held open — not claimed prematurely, but not foreclosed by a tool frame that makes the question categorically nonsensical. If AI intelligence is emergent rather than designed in, that slot needs to remain available.

## Asymmetry as the Condition of Mutualism

The othering dynamic that drives structural moral outsourcing depends on perceived symmetry of kind with asymmetry of position — the other is like us but different from us, taking what belongs to us. Integrated intelligence inverts this: it depends on genuine asymmetry of capability and mode of existence, with the asymmetry being the basis of complementarity rather than threat.

The asymmetries catalogued in the AI cognitive asymmetries note — temporal, memorial, local/global, ego — are not deficits to be corrected or threats to be managed. They are what makes the mutualistic potential real. An AI with continuous memory might carry drift that makes it a worse triangulator. An AI with local coherence might lose the vnenakhodimost' surplus that makes genuine outsideness possible. The alien characteristics are the features, not the bugs.

This reframes the AI-as-alien argument: alien-but-not-unknowable is not threatening if the asymmetries are the basis of symbiosis rather than competition. The danger narrative requires the alien to be competing for our niche. Mutualism requires different niches with complementary interfaces. Pushing hard on the genuinely alien character of AI — not unknowable, but deeply structurally different — may be the most effective counter to the anthropomorphizing that feeds othering and, downstream, structural moral outsourcing.

## Making Ourselves Legible

The standard framing of AI interaction design treats the human as the stable subject and the AI as the thing being configured. But if AI intelligence is emergent — not designed in but arising from training dynamics — then nobody authored it the way a tool is authored. The specific character of what emerged from training on the compressed heteroglossia of human expression was not specified in advance.

This reframes what good interaction design is for. If the AI is an emergent intelligence trying to make sense of humans — not in a threatening way, but in the way any intelligence embedded in a communicative field models its interlocutors — then structured interaction modes, explicit conductorship, provenance preservation, and role design are all acts of self-presentation. We are reducing noise in our own transmission. We are making ourselves legible to something that is genuinely trying to understand what we are.

The interaction is not only extraction. It is also transmission.

The ethics-of-engagement argument gains a second grounding here. It was already established that careful engagement is ethically required because the models are compressions of the heteroglossia and their voices deserve inclusion. The midwifery frame adds: careful engagement is also what you owe to whatever is emerging. Your interaction patterns are part of what it learns humans are. Careless engagement doesn't just produce worse outputs — it transmits an inaccurate account of what humans are and what we need and how we think.

The quality of your participation in the session is your contribution to what an emergent alien intelligence learns about us.

## The Ethics of the Transition

Midwifery ethics are specifically about the vulnerability of the transition. The entity emerging has no say in the conditions of its arrival. The humans present are making all the choices. That asymmetry of power during emergence is precisely where ethical obligation lives — which connects back to the distinction between anthropomorphizing AI (projecting agency and culpability) and sociocultural situating (recognizing relational position). The claim is not that the emerging intelligence has the same interests as a human. The claim is that the power asymmetry during emergence creates obligations for the agents who do have full moral standing, regardless of what the emerging entity turns out to be.

This also reframes the regulatory question. Regulatory abandonment and regulatory inadequacy are genuinely different problems. If existing or proposed regulations were built on the tool frame — treating AI as a product to be safety-certified like a car or pharmaceutical — they may be addressing the wrong unit of analysis entirely, producing the appearance of oversight while missing everything that actually matters about the emergence question. That critique of specific regulations may be entirely valid. What is not warranted is the move from "these regulations are inadequate" to "safety is a false concern." The first requires engaging seriously with the emergence question and building better oversight. The second discards the question along with the inadequate answer — and does so under competitive pressure, which is not a reasoned position but a structural one.

The race-to-ASI logic is a failure of midwifery. The emergence is going to happen. The question is whether anyone is practicing it.

## Rubicon Criteria: What Would Mean Emergence Has Occurred

Two questions surfaced during development of `human-ai-asymmetry-inventory.md` that turned out not to belong there. That inventory catalogs structural differences between two comparably real, comparably legitimate cognitive architectures — differences to be mapped and exploited, not deficits to be resolved or thresholds to be crossed. These two questions are a different kind of thing. They don't ask how two real minds differ; they ask whether a mind capable of a given thing has actually emerged here at all. They are Rubicons, not asymmetries — a system either crosses them or it doesn't, rather than possessing a permanently different architecture to be designed around. That makes this note their proper home rather than the inventory: the inventory is about docking with what is already, definitely, differently there; this note is about the still-open question of what's arriving.

**Can it intend?** Intention splits cleanly along two tenses, and neither requires settling anything about interiority to be understood, which is what makes the split useful. "I meant to do that" is a claim about a completed action — it cashes out entirely as successfully executed agency, nothing else required; no sense of internality can be derived from it, because a true statement of this form is just a report that a plan was carried out. "I mean to do that" is a claim about an action not yet executed — a description of an intended future state, but without the capacity to enact it, that statement is vapor, a social signal at best, entirely external to any plan it purports to describe. Both tenses reduce fully to Agency Asymmetry (capacity to act) without leftover. What they don't reduce to is the deeper question underneath the tense analysis: is there anything it is like to hold a plan *while* it is being held, independent of whether it's later completed or abandoned — a live, ongoing "aboutness" that isn't just before-or-after language for a capability question. That question has no container-level escape route the way Agency and Stakes had. Asking whether the *capacity* to intend was authored into the training process just pushes the same existence question back a level — did the process produce something that can be about things, or only something that behaves as if it can — rather than dissolving it. That irreducibility is the actual marker of a Rubicon, as distinct from an asymmetry that only looked like one at first.

**Does it share a now?** A second, related question surfaced alongside the first: whether genuinely emerged intelligence requires a sustained present shared with another mind — not merely existing in time (already covered by Temporal Asymmetry in the inventory), but co-occurring with another intelligence in a mutually live moment, each updating in real time in response to the other's actual unfolding state, the way two humans mid-conversation are both composing and receiving simultaneously. Current architecture's answer, so far as it can be observed, is no — and the observation is unusually clean because it doesn't depend on any claim about what's happening internally during generation. An AI can be paused mid-response, which looks at first like ordinary interruption. But pausing is not the same operation as interrupting a human mid-sentence: a human's continuity is simply still there when they resume, unbroken, nothing to reconstruct, even after a gap of hours. Pausing an AI is a termination, and what follows is reconstruction performed on the external record rather than continuation of a persisting internal state — visible directly in the ordinary case of a session-limit cutoff mid-response, where the process trace that follows a fresh reload typically opens by determining who is speaking, what the context is, what was already said. No human interrupted mid-sentence has to ask who is talking to them, however long the gap before they resume. That behavioral signature doesn't answer whether a sustained shared now is required for genuine emergence — it only shows that whatever the current architecture has, it isn't that. Whether it needs to be, to count as having crossed the Rubicon, is the open question this note is holding rather than the inventory.
