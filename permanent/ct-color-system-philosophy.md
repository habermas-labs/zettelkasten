## The Tetrahedron as Structural Metaphor

Crosstalk Lab's visual identity centers on a regular tetrahedron — four vertices, six edges, four equilateral triangular faces. The choice of this specific geometry is not aesthetic. It is a diagram of the theoretical argument underlying CT.

Each vertex represents a situated epistemic position:
- **Claude** (amber) — one model horizon
- **ChatGPT** (green) — a second model horizon
- **Gemini** (blue) — a third model horizon
- **User** (cool white) — the human horizon; integrating presence

The object under examination in any CT session is the implicit center of the tetrahedron — visible differently from each vertex, never completely from any one.

This is the hermeneutic argument made geometric. See `sociocultural-hermeneutics.md`.

---

## Why a Regular Tetrahedron

The regularity of the tetrahedron — all edges equal, all faces equilateral triangles — carries philosophical content. It enforces the claim that no vertex is structurally superior to any other. The models and the user occupy equivalent positions in the geometry of the argument.

An irregular tetrahedron (e.g., with isosceles rather than equilateral faces, or one vertex elevated by geometry rather than perspective) would smuggle in a hierarchy the design philosophy explicitly refuses. CT's claim is that integrated intelligence requires genuine equivalence of positions, not a privileged center.

### The Perspective Problem

Any fixed 2D rendering of the tetrahedron will appear to privilege whichever vertex is at the "top" of the viewing angle. This is an artifact of the projection, not a property of the shape. The logo should be understood as a snapshot of a 3D object that has no inherent top.

The interactive tetrahedron concept — a rotatable 3D wireframe for public-facing CT documentation — is the experiential argument for this point. A user who rotates the model discovers that "top" is a camera convention. This could also be demonstrated through animation: open on the fixed view, rotate slowly to show the equivalence of all four vertices.

## The Color System

### Palette A — Brand-Anchored (current implementation)

Colors were chosen to maintain association with each model's brand identity while functioning harmoniously against the CT interface's deep navy background (`#0a0e17`).

| Vertex | Role | Cool (vertex) | Warm (edge midpoint) |
|--------|------|---------------|----------------------|
| Claude | Amber | `#d4a574` | `#e8c090` |
| ChatGPT | Green | `#6bcf8e` | `#a0e8b0` |
| Gemini | Blue | `#7ba4f4` | `#a0c0f8` |
| User | Cool white | `#d8dfe8` | `#e8e0d4` |

### The Cool/Warm Vertex-to-Edge Distinction

Each vertex is rendered in a cool version of its color. Each edge — connecting two vertices of different colors — carries a double gradient: hue transitions between the two endpoint colors, and temperature transitions from cool at the vertices toward warm at the midpoint.

The warm midpoint suggests the generative zone between positions — the boundary where, in Bakhtinian terms, meaning arises. The vertices are the situated positions; the edges are where the productive gap lives. The warmth is not ambient — it is generated by the epistemic friction between the perspectives meeting at that boundary.

### The User as White

The user vertex is a cool blue-tinted white (`#d8dfe8`), warming toward paper white (`#e8e0d4`) at edge midpoints. White carries the conceptual argument that the user is the presence of all colors — not independent of the other positions but integrating them. The tinted rather than pure white suggests a human presence that has been *shaped* by the triangulation, not one that stands neutrally above it.

The additive/subtractive color argument: blue and green are additive primaries; amber sits adjacent to the subtractive primaries. All three model colors are partial. White suggests the completion of the set — but a slightly warm, slightly cool white, not a pure theoretical white. The human vertex is not a completion in the mathematical sense; it is a synthesizing presence.

## The Wireframe Rendering

- **Background:** `#0a0e17` — CT interface background, deep navy near-black
- **Edges:** gradient strokes per the color system above
- **Glow:** soft warm glow in the edge's midpoint color — bioluminescent rather than neon
- **Vertices:** small solid dots in the cool vertex color
- **Faces:** unfilled — the tetrahedron is a wireframe, not a solid. The faces are implied, not stated.

The wireframe treatment reinforces the philosophical point: CT does not claim to contain or fill in the space between perspectives. It traces the structure of the relationships.

---

## Logo and Favicon

- **Primary logo:** 500×500px, square crop, tetrahedron floating centered against background
- **Favicon:** reduced from the same composition; vertex dots and glow should remain legible at small sizes
- **SVG preferred** for scalability, though gradient/glow effects may require raster fallback

### Perspective Specification

Orient with one vertex at top-rear, base triangle at bottom-front, rotated approximately 15-20° off-axis so all six edges are simultaneously visible. No edge should be hidden behind another face.
