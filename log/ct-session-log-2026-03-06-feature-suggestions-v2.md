## Thread Arc

The session opened with public-facing documentation: elevator speech drafts (spoken and email versions) and a PDF pipeline using reportlab. During review, Skooter noted that the pitch was triangulation-focused — a collapsed Tetra — and that fully enabling the fourth vertex where the human sits was the move that makes CT genuinely distinctive. This observation seeded the dimensionality ladder.

The ladder emerged as a clean pitch scaffold: 1D (linear exchange) → 2D (triangulation) → 2D+ (conducted triangulation through directed participation) → 3D (Tetras). Each step a genuine dimensional expansion, not just "more."

From there the session moved into interaction geometry — specifically geometries that AI enables that are either impossible or prohibitively contrived for human communication. The Crosstalk move (simultaneous bilateral exchange without signal loss) was the clearest example, arising from the observation that human communication is necessarily asymmetric due to signal loss, ego investment, social contamination, and role exclusivity. AI removes or relaxes all of these simultaneously.

This produced the interaction geometries whitepaper thesis: not "AI does human things faster" but "AI enables interaction structures with no meaningful human analog."

The Hedra concept emerged from thinking about structured interaction presets defined by the facing each model turns toward the problem. Skooter coined the term from the Greek root *hedra* (face) — the same root in polyhedron naming — establishing Hedra as a parallel class to Tetras. Tetras define who is at each vertex; Hedras define what face each model turns toward the object of inquiry. The two classes are orthogonal and composable.

The session closed with the observation that geometry metaphors are doing two distinct kinds of work in CT — dimensionality (depth of interaction) and polyhedra (shape of configurations) — warranting separate permanent notes for each.

## Key Observations Not Captured Elsewhere

**The pitch as collapsed Tetra:** The elevator speech as written positions the human as moderator of a three-way AI conversation — accurate for the entry point but underselling the full geometry. The fourth vertex move (human as structurally load-bearing participant rather than external conductor) is the thing that makes CT genuinely novel. Leaving it implicit in the pitch is strategically useful — it invites the question.

**Two Claude.ai usability findings relevant to Drew Bent conversation:** (1) Learning Mode buried three interactions deep under the plus button — primary affordance is file attachment, not behavioral configuration. (2) "Copy" button in artifact preview hides Download, Download as PDF, and Add to Project — functionality mislabeled and hidden. Both are violations of Nielsen's visibility heuristic and Krug's discoverability principle. Two concrete examples from direct product experience, directly relevant to Drew's educational use case goals.

**Hexahedron as Hedra naming candidate:** A hexahedron has 6 faces, which maps to the 3x2 Perspective Matrix (each of 3 models argues 2 positions = 6 response faces). The geometry may be naming the Hedras.

**Porosity as the right name:** "Welcome to porosity" hits strange in exactly the right way for a Tetra designed around productive discomfort and opening. The strangeness is the point — it causes stopping and considering before the session begins. The name does the work before the session starts.

**RIFF defined more precisely:** Reflect, Interrogate, Fold Forward. The culinary fold metaphor is preferred over the blanket fold — folding in cooking preserves the structure of both ingredients while making them part of a new whole. Neither overwrites the other. This is the correct epistemic analog for what RIFF's third move does.
